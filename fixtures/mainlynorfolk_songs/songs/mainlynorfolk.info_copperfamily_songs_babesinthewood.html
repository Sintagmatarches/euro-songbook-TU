Babes in the Wood / Lost in a Wood

Babes in the Wood is a song from the repertoire of the Copper Family. It originated from a story dating back to 1601 or before but the version sung today was written by William Gardiner early in the nineteenth century. Bob and Ron Copper sang it on their 1963 EFDSS LP Traditional Songs From Rottingdean; this recording was included in 2001 on their Topic compilation of early recordings of the Copper Family, Come Write Me Down. The Copper Family also sang this in 1971 on their Leader album A Song for Every Season, and they sang it at Will Nobleâs barn in Denby Dale, Yorkshire on 27 September 1986. The recordings from that day were released a year later on the VWML cassette Willâs Barn. And they recorded it again in 1998 for their CD Coppersongs 3: The Legacy Continues.

However, the present song was composed early in the 19th century by William Gardiner (1770-1853), who was born in Leicester. Today Gardiner is best known for the hymns that he composed, including The Lordâs My Shepherd.

Dorothy Howard from Texas sang three verses of Babes in the Wood to Anne and Frank Warner in Cooperstown, Nw York in 1949. This recording was included in 2000 on the Appleseed anthology of songs from the Warner Collection, Her Bright Smile Haunts Me Still. They noted:

This is a ballad that goes back at least to the 1500s. It is in Pepys Diary of the 1660s, and Dr. Samuel Johnson knew it well enough to parody. It is this fragment, however that has survived in tradition, widely, on both sides of the Atlantic.

Shirley Collins sang Babes in the Wood in 1967 on her album The Sweet Primeroses with the Young Tradition joining in on the chorus. She commented in the albumâs notes on the Copperâs legacy:

After the Copper family lost their sheep-farm on the Sussex downs to suburban speculators, their songs were just about all they had left. Gordon Hall sang Babes in the Wood in a private recording from 1994 that was included in 2001 on his posthumous CountryBranch album Good Things Enough. Jim Ward noted:

The sad tale of small children left to die in a wood first appeared as a broadside ballad in 1595. It was widely reprinted and Joseph Addison described it in 1711 in The Spectator as “one of the Darling Songs of the Common People, … he Delight of most Englishmen in some Part of their Age”. Thomas Percy accorded it the honour, under the title of The Children in the Wood, of inclusion in his prestigious Reliques of Ancient English Poetry (1765). Early in the nineteenth century, Johnny Pitts, the London ballad printer; issued and edition with each of the twenty verses illustrated by its own woodcut. Despite such exposure, however, it was not this version but a shortened remake by William Gardiner (1770-1853) of Leicester which survived into recent oral tradition (including, once more, the Coppers).
