George ‘Pop’ Maynard sang The Sweet Nightingale (The Birds in the Spring) at home in Copthorne, Sussex, in 1955, where he was recorded by Peter Kennedy for the BBC. This Peter Kennedy was released in 2012 on the Topic anthology of songs by Southern English traditional singers, You Never Heard So Sweet (The Voice of the People Volume 21). Bob and John Copper sang this song with the title By the Green Grove in 1971 on their 4 LP Leader box set, A Song for Every Season. Bob, John, Jill and Lynne Copper and Jon Dudley sang it again in 1987 on the anthology LP Square Roots: An Unlikely Selection From the Pages of Folk Roots Magazine, and Bob, Jill and John Copper and Jon Dudley sang it in 1995 on their CD Coppersongs 2: The Living Tradition of the Copper Family. Shirley Collins noted on the 2012 Topic anthology:

Cousins Bob and Ron Copper of the famous singing Sussex family—one that can be traced back through the Rottingdean parish records to 1593. Coppers had lived in that one corner of Sussex since that time, earning their livings as shepherds, farm workers and publicans, loving and appreciating their country life. The feeling that runs through this tender yet sturdy song, is best expressed by Bob Copper in his book A Song for Every Season (London, Heinemann, 1971): “They were brought up in close contact with nature and the moods of the sea and the wiles of the weather were all around them. The flowers in the field and the birds of the air were their daily companions, and all the time the peaceful and patient tenor of life seemed to penetrate even the thickest of exteriors and leave some kind of mark on their hearts.” The Copper familyâs early recordings and family history are dealt with comprehensively in Come Write Me Down.

Goblin Band sang Birds in the Spring in 2024 on their Broadside Hacks EP Come Slack Your Horse!. They noted:

18th century in origin, Birds in the Spring is situated at a time when vast swathes of England were privately owned by the aristocracy, and access to common land was becoming increasingly eroded through the violence of enclosure, From a time when people were still living closer to the rhythms of the seasons and yet alienated from the access they once had within living memory. Te subject calls the listener to notice the birdsong, which can be read as a lamentation, a remnant of a fading and mutating connection to the landscape.
