Outward Bound / Homeward Bound

[ Roud 1104 / Song Subject MAS911 ; Master title: Outward Bound ; Ballad Index Hugi541 ; VWML RoudFS/S157339 , RVW2/3/101 , SBG/1/2/305 ; Bodleian Roud 1104 ; DT HOMEBND , OUTHOME2 ; Mudcat 51420 ; trad.]

According to Steve Gardham on Mudcat, this song “may well have become a forebitter but it was written by William Upton for the stage in about 1790. See the Universal Songster.”

Ralph Vaughan Williams collected Our Anchor芒聙聶s Weighed in January 1905 from Mrs Betty Howard of King芒聙聶s Lynn, Norfolk [VWML RVW2/3/101] . Roy Palmer included this version, using the title Homeward Bound, in his 1983 book Folk Songs Collected by Ralph Vaughan Williams. Palmer noted:

“In sailing ship days this song was a prime favourite, and was sung all the world over”, wrote Captain W.B. Whall (Sea Songs and Shanties, 1910, p. 5). The name of the docks mentioned varies with the home port of the vessel, but the Dog and Bell public house remains constant. This is one of the very few shanties to have been printed on broadsides. Unusually, too, for a shanty, this version was sung by a woman, Mrs Betty Howard.

Alan Helsdon in his 2014 digital book Vaughan Williams in Norfolk has a version of Homeward Bound that in the main text is attributed to Mrs Howard, but in the sources section says that, while the tune and verses 4 and 6-7 are from Mrs Howard, verses 1-3 ad 5 are from Frank Kidson芒聙聶s book Traditional Tunes.

The Ian Campbell Folk Group sang Homeward Bound on their 1963 album This Is the Ian Campbell Folk Group.

Cyril Tawney sang Outward Bound in 1963 on his Argo album A Mayflower Garland. He noted:

This ditty from the days of sail says much the same as the modern American song Nobody Needs You When You芒聙聶re Down and Out. At the end of a voyage a sailor was a comparatively wealthy man in a seaport and friends were easy to come by, friends who steadily evaporated as the ‘chink’ disappeared. Soon it was time to “go to sea for more”, a situation so common that in time the phrase ‘outward bound’ actually came to mean ‘broke’ among sailors.

Our version here was collected by the Rev. S. Baring-Gould from Will Huggins of Lydford, Devon [VWML SBG/1/2/305] .
