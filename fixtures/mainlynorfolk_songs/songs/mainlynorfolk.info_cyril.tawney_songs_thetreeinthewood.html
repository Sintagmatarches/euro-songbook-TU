Maud Long of Hot Springs, Madison County, North Carolina sang The Tree in the Wood to Maud Karpeles on 27 September 1950 and Mrs Martha Wiseman Aldridge of Three Mile, Avery County, North Carolina sang Pretty Fair Field to Maud Karpeles in 1950 too. Both recordings were included in 2017 on the Musical Traditions anthology of historic recordings of Appalachian singers and musicians 1927-1955, When Cecil Left the Mountains. Rod Stradling noted:

The Tree in the Wood has often been collected from folksingers, not only in Britain, but in France, Denmark and Switzerland as well. The song is also called The Everlasting Circle and, in this form, the feather becomes a bed, a maiden lies on the bed, a youth sleeps with the maiden, a child is born who grows to plant an acorn which becomes a tree, thus completing the circle. Another Appalachian singer, Martha Wiseman Aldridge, can be heard singing her version of the song on CD2 of this set.

John Casley of Morvah, Cornwall sang this song as The Green Grass Grew All Around, accompanying himself on the church organ, in a recording made by Peter Kennedy on 19 November 1956. This BBC recording 23654 was included on the anthology Animal Songs (The Folk Songs of Britain Volume 10; Caedmon 1961, Topic 1970). The albumâs booklet noted:

A broadside by Pitts of Seven Dials (n.d.) has “feather, bed, maid and man” as the final sequence.

A Danish version, Langt udi skoven (Long out in the wood) is published in Danmarks Melodier described as a Sang-Renise (Rigmarole type song). A Swiss version, Dert undenider on dert steit e Birliboum, in Kinderiied und Kinderspiel im Kanton Bern G. Zuricher, begins with the tree and ends with the pip within the core.

A Welsh version Ar y bryn death pren, (The Journal of the Welsh Folk-Song Society vol. i no. i) ends (translated):

The bed from the feathers
The feathers from the chicken
The chicken from the egg
The egg from the nest
The nest on the branch
The branch on the tree
The tree on the earth
And the earth on nothing.

The Welsh versions conform to the Celtic patterns of internal assonantal rhyme and perhaps hint that the Breton and Welsh forms are the oldest. John Casleyâs ancesters may have performed the song in the Cornish language.

Johnny Casley, like his father from whom he learnt this song, is a farmer and church organist. His father played in the local church for 46 years and John has already been at it 40. He has also been chapel organist for 67 years and played trombone in Pendeen band for 25. Apart from the last war, when he was in the “Home Guard”, he has never missed a Sunday.

Johnny said this song was known “right through Pendeen” (district). As a boy, he recalls, he used to meet the rest of the lads outside the church and they would have a sing-song of songs of just this sort.

This recording by Mike Yates was published in 1976 on the Topic album Green Grow the Laurels: Country Singers From the South, and was included in 2001 on the Musical Traditions anthology of songs from the Mike Yates collection, Up in the North and Down in the South. Mike Yates noted on the original album:

Cyril Poacher sang The Bog Down in the Valley at home on Grove Farm, Blaxhall, Suffolk on 4 August 1974. This recording by Tony Engle was published a year later on Poacherâs Topic album The Broomfield Wager: Traditional Songs From Suffolk, and was included in 2004 on his Musical Traditions anthology Plenty of Thyme Rod Stradling noted:

Cyril says he learnt The Bog Down in the Valley from his mother, Alice Ling, in Sing, Say or Pay!, but tells Ginette Dunn he learned it from his grandfather. It is, of course, a version of the well known Everlasting Circle, which is scattered throughout Europe and most English versions follow the pattern set by John Pitts who printed the song in the early 1800âs. Cyril, however, seems to have the Irish set of words.

Judy Cook sang Tree in the Woods on her 1998 album If You Sing Songs…. She noted:

From Sharp, English Folk Songs From the Southern Appalachians, sung by Mrs Jane Gentry at Hot Springs, North Carolina, 9 December 1916, last three items from “The Beautiful Tree” sung by an unknown singer on Maud Karpelesâ Appalachian collection.

I love the feeling of timeless potentiality of the unborn song on the tongue of the bird in the egg. This song is a melding of two similar songs from a recording of Appalachia the Old Traditions that Julie Henigan gave me.

The Witches of Elswick sang The Tree in the Wood in 2003 on their first album, Out of Bed. They noted:

Otherwise known as The Everlasting Circle, this cumulative song usually includes copulation, but we thought that was too rude so our bird just flies away. Maybe we missed the point of the song but we liked it anyway.
