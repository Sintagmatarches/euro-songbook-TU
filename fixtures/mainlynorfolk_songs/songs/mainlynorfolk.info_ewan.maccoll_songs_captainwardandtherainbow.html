The Clutha sang Captain Ward and the Reindeer in 1971 on their Argo album Scotia!. They noted:

Captain John Ward was an English buccaneer who. with a Dutch accomplice Dansekar, practised piracy on the high seas during the period 1604-9. This version was collected by Gavin Greig from Miss L. Crighton of Bonnykelly, Aberdeenshire. Two other versions in fragmentary form were found by Greig in the same county. The ballad would appear to be English in origin, but how it found its way to the North East of Scotland is not known.

Roy Harris sang Captain Ward in 1975 on his Topic album Champions of Folly. This track was also included on the Topic compilation CD Round Cape Horn. A.L. Lloyd noted on the original album:

John Ward of Kent was one of the most daring of pirates who took refuge in the ports of the Barbary Coast in the opening years of the 17th century. It was European renegades such as Ward and his ‘Dutch’ partner Danseker (Danziger?) who taught the Moors the pirate trade and helped to establish the port of Sallee (nowadays spelled Sale) near Rabat as a centre for sea robbers prowling up to the Straits of Gibraltar. Ballads about Ward were already circulating in 1603, and a play based on his misdeeds, called A Christian Turned Turk, was presented in 1612. The name of the ship Rainbow is possibly a confusion with the name of the bold Captain [Rainsborough]. The tune is one that Ewan MacColl learnt from his father. Roy Harris heard Ewan sing the ballad, liked the tune, “looked up some words, and here we are”.

Alba sang Captain Ward in 1978 on their eponymous album on the Rubber Records label, Alba.

Tundra sang Captain Ward and the Rainbow in 1978 on their Sweet Folk and Country album A Kentish Garland. They noted:

The earliest reference to this alleged incident is the registration in 1609 of a ballad slip entitled “A Famous Sea Fight Between Captain Ward and the Rainbow”. It could be that the Queen referred to in the song is Elizabeth I but it is more likely that the story has been updated in this particular version and that it is Queen Victoria. In any event, John Ward was a sea captain from Faversham who defied not only Royal Authority but the British Navy as well to emerge a triumphant privateer.
