… and Steve Roud noted on the Topic anthology:

Often collected in Scotland and England, but only a handful of times in North America, The Jolly Beggar is one of several ballads with a beggar or tinker as the main character. Lucy Stewart芒聙聶s version is a little too short to make the whole story clear, although her listeners probably knew what it was all about. A beggar comes to the door of a house and requests lodging for the night, which is granted. When the daughter of the house ‘gets up to bar the door’, he seduces her, although it is not always clear how willing she is. He then cuts off his beggar芒聙聶s rags, to reveal his fine clothes, and when he blows his horn ‘five-and-twenty belted knights’ answer his call. But the story varies considerably from version to version, and there has been a degree of cross-fertilisation with other ‘beggar’ songs. The key distinguishing point here being whether he is really a noble dressed as a beggar, or is actually a beggar and fools the girl into thinking he is a nobleman in disguise.

The earliest known versions of The Jolly Beggar are all Scottish, and date from the mid-18th century. There have long been persistent traditions that certain Scottish kings enjoyed going amongst their people disguised as ordinary folk, or even as beggars, and also that the present ballad was written by James V. These traditions are perpetuated by ballad-editors who repeat them uncritically, and there is always a problem when writers, who should know better, begin to believe the legends which they are reporting. Professor Child芒聙聶s elegant dismissal of the idea of a royal author, which he says, “has no more plausibility than it has authority”, can be extended to the whole notion of kings disguised as beggars. Child is uncharacteristically reticent when it comes to deciding the origin of the ballad, and hedges his bets as to whether it is Scottish or English. He points to a black-letter broadside in the Pepys Collection, printed in London in the 1650s, entitled The Pollitick Begger-Man, which also concerns a beggar getting lodging and seducing the daughter of the house. But although there are a few definite textual similarities with the earlier versions of The Jolly Beggar, this story is quite different. The English song is directed to be sung to the tune of There Was a Jolly Beggar, but this is yet another song, which simply extols the pleasures of a begging life.
