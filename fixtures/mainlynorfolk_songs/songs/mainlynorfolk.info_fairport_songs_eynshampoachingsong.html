Dave and Toni Arthur sang The Eynsham Poaching Song in 1967 on their Transatlantic album Morning Stands on Tiptoe. They noted:

Between 1788-1867, over 137,000 convicts were transported to Australia, Tasmania and Norfolk Island for various minor offences including poaching. The severity of the laws however did little to stop the poachers. In the above song the poachers are lucky and get back home to tell the tale and the song ends on a note of defiance. Staverton Bridge sang the Eynsham Poaching Song in 1975 on their eponymous Saydisc album Staverton Bridge.

Canterbury Fair sang The Eynsham Poaching Song on their eponymous 1977 album Canterbury Fair. They noted:

During the last few years, hoe of the great joys for anyone interested in the folk songs of Britain has been the publication of a number of books on the subject by Roy Palmer. It was in his book Room for Company (Cambridge University Press, 1971) that we found this superb poaching song. Fairport Convention sang The Eynsham Poacher on their 1977 album The Bonny Bunch of Roses. This track was also included on their 1998 anthology Fiddlestix: The Best of Fairport 1972-1984. A live recording from Cropredy 1982 was released in 1996 on their Musikfolk video Forever Young. A live recording from Cropredy 1983 was included in the following year on their Woodworm cassette The Boot.

Magpie Lane played the fiddle tune Johnny So Long and sang the Eynsham Poaching Song in 1993 on their Beautiful Jo album of songs and tunes from Oxfordshire, The Oxford Ramble. They noted:

The fiddle tune is the Bampton Morris version of a popular English folk melody and accompanies a handkerchief dance. The fiddle played here belonged to Sam Bennett who was with the Bampton Morris 60-odd years ago. It was kindly loaned to us by Arnold Woodley, also a well known Bampton fiddler. The tune leads directly into the Eynsham Poaching Song, still popular in Eynsham. This was obtained by Alfred Williams whose Folk Songs of the Upper Thames (1923) remains an important source for traditional song in the Oxfordshire area.
