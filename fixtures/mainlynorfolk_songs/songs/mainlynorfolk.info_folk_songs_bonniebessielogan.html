Bonnie Bessie Logan is a poem in Alexander Andersonâs book Later Poems of Alexander Anderson, “Surfaceman” (Glasgow: Frasher, Asher & Co., 1913, p.115-6).

John Mearns sang Bonnie Bessie Logan in ca 1964-65 on his Scottish Records EP Folk-Songs of the North-East. Producer Douglas Gray noted:

Little is known of Bonnie Bessie Logan. This version was taken down from an old lady in Montrose by John Mearns himself. The lady, who was eighty, said that she heard her mother sing it sixty or seventy years before.

Ian Manuel sang Bonnie Bessie Logan on his 1977 Topic album of Scots traditional songs, The Dales of Caledonia. A.L. Lloyd noted:

Scotland has been specially prolific in lyrical songs that lie close to folk tradition but are nonetheless the compositions of educated author, whether amateur or professional, anonymous or known. This is one such, with stylised rural poetry set to a good tune that, brisked up, has also served as a march and has been adapted for one or two music hall ditties. Ian Manuel learnt it from the singing of the good Aberdeenshire singer John Mearns.

Alex Campbell sang Bonnie Bessie Logan in 1978 on his Sweet Folk All album Traditional Ballads of Scotland. He noted:

The real backbone of the British folk revival is the resident singers of the clubs who, each weak, give their time and effort unstintingly. One such group is the Minor Birds in Warrington from whom I learned this song. I added a fourth verse only to learn about a year later when doing a TV show with Ian Campbell that his father Davy sings a five verse version of the song.

Bram Taylor sang Bonnie Bessie Logan in 1993 on his Fellside CD Further Horizons. He noted:

A traditional song which I have had for some time (grateful thanks to Paul [Adams] who found an extra verse for me). A beautiful song which may ring true to some men who have reached their middle age crisis!

A long time favourite of mine, this elusive song was learned from Arthur Argo many years ago. All trails lead back to Aberdeenshire where I look for information.
