Archie Fisher sang Echo Mocks the Corncrake in 1976 on his Folk-Legacy album The Man With a Rhyme. He also sang Corncrake in the same year on the fundraiser album The Second Folk Review Record. He noted:

An Ayrshire love song that has more of the drawing room about it than the tradition. Unclaimed by Burns, the melody more than justifies its place in the Scottish lolksinger芒聙聶s repertoire. I borrowed it from Geordie Hamilton of Edinburgh.

Billy Ross sang The Corncrake in 1977 on Ossian芒聙聶s eponymous Springthyme album Ossian. They noted:

A traditional love song, The Corncrake comes from south west Scotland—mentioning the town of Ayr and the river Doune which flows to the sea just south of the town. The sound of the corncrake (a harsh rasping call which might not be thought particularly romantic) has now become rare in mainland Scotland with the loss of meadow habitat. The song is known throughout Scotland and is in the Greig-Duncan Folk-Song Collection.

Kentigern sang The Corncrake in 1979 on their eponymous Topic album Kentigern. This track was also included in 1996 on the Topic sampler Scottish Voices. They noted:

A beautiful love song from Ayrshire. The bird, though in danger of extinction, can still be found in the Western Isles.

Tam Reid sang The Corncraik Amang the Whinney in a 1988 recording made at Towie Barclay Castle that was first released on a cassette and in 2003 on his anthology Behind the Bothy Door, Volume 2.

Heather Heywood sang The Corncrake Amang the Whinnie Knowes in 1993 on her Greentrax CD By Yon Castle Wa芒聙聶. She noted:

An attractive song with somebody remembering a courtship in what is a beautiful area of countryside, Ayrshire. “The best things in life are free.” So says the writer comparing the fancy perfumes with the pleasure and beauty of nature.

Andy M. Stewart sang The Echo Mocks the Corncrake in 1994 on his Green Linnet album Man in the Moon. He noted:

The corncrake is a bird whose mechanical-sounding call was once a common sound throughout rural Scotland, but is now found only in parts of the Western Isles. I first heard this song at family ceilidhs when I was a child and loved its spirit. It extols the virtue and worth of a simple life close to nature and the land.

Deaf Shepherd sang The Corncraik in 1997 on their Greentrax album Synergy.

Ellen Mitchell sang Echo Mocks the Corncrake, “[a]n Ayrshire song that I learned from the singing of the late Joe Holmes from Co Antrim, Ireland”, in 2001 on her and Kevin Mitchell芒聙聶s Musical Traditions anthology Have a Drop Mair. Rod Stradling commented in the album芒聙聶s booklet:

Emily Smith sang Corncrake Among the Winnie Knowes on her 2013 anniversary album Ten Years.

Jim Malcolm sang The Echo Mocks the Corncrake on his 2014 album The Corncrake. He noted:

Though the author is unknown, references to the River Doon and Ayr made me think Burns might have had a hand in this lovely song, which can be found in Robert Ford芒聙聶s Vagabond Songs and Ballads of Scotland, published in 1905.

Janice Burns and Jon Doran sang The Corncrake on their 2022 CD No More the Green Hills. They noted:

Janice found a fragment of Sheila Stewart singing the first few verses of this and was immediately taken with her ability to draw the listener into her story. The message we took from it was one of freedom, and the importance of access to nature for everyone. With the tightening of trespassing laws, this is no longer a possibility for most people and is having devastating consequences for Gypsy, Roma and Traveller communities.
