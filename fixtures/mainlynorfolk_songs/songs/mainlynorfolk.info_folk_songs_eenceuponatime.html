Jeannie Robertson sang Eenst Upon a Time in 1959 on her EMI/HMV EP of tender and ribald songs of Scotland, Jeannie芒聙聶s Merry Muse. Another version, recorded live with audience participation by Hamish Henderson in Edinburgh in 1958, was included in 1984 on her posthumous album Up the Dee and Doon the Don. Hamish Henderson noted on the first album:

The plight of the forsaken girl whose lover has abandoned her has inspired many beautiful Scots love-laments (e.g. the most famous version of Waly Waly), but in Eenst Upon a Time we see the other side of the medal—tough, ironic, unsentimental. The servant lassie who has a child by the boy she has led on, thinks of him with native humour, and without rancour.

Ray Fisher sang Aince Upon a Time, accompanied by her brother Archie on guitar, on the Fisher Family芒聙聶s 1966 Topic album Traditional & New Songs From Scotland. Norman Buchan noted on The Birken Tree and on this song:

These two tell you all you need to know about Scottish folk-song. The clear narrative line in the first, the trysting place as always the birken tree (the birch); and the couthy kitchie background of the second, clear, direct and hard, and in both the swinging belt of the tune with the floating last notes. What more is to be said? Except that the last song was learned from Jeannie Robertson and added to by Ray Fisher and this perhaps symbolises their true response to the traditional; that the best way of respecting it is by developing it.

Gordeanna McCulloch sang Eence Upon a Time on her 1978 Topic album with The Clutha, Sheath and Knife. She commented in her sleeve notes:

From the singing of Jeannie Robertson of Aberdeen with additional verses (2, 3, 4 and 6) by Ray Fisher. Published in Norman Buchan and Peter Hall芒聙聶s The Scottish Folksinger (London & Glasgow, 1973).

Christine Stewart sang Aince Upon a Time in 1998 on the Foundry Bar Band album Rolling Home.

Maureen Jelks sang Eence Upon a Time (When I Wis Young and Bonny) in 2000 as the title track of her Tradition Bearers album Eence Upon a Time. She noted:

First heard from the singing of Ray Fisher, one of my favourite singers. To be found in The Scottish Folksinger. Verses 1, 5 and 7 are from the original version by Jeannie Robertson, the others were added by Ray. It is a song of warning to young girls when working at the threshing, to

Be sure ye gaither in the grain,
And not the chaff that blaws.

Ellen Mitchell sang Aince Upon a Time in 2002 on her Tradition Bearers album On Yonder Lea. She noted:

This is a song I have heard over many years, and sung by many singers. I liked it but was never inspired to learn it until I heard the late Christine Stewart singing it on the Foundry Bar Band CD Rolling Home. She lifted the song to another dimension for me. I greatly admire her interpretation and style.
