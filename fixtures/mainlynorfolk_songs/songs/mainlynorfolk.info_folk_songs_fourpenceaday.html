Fourpence a Day

[ Roud 2586 ; Master title: Fourpence a Day ; TYG 107 ; Ballad Index FSWB130A ; Folkinfo 708 ; DT FOURPENC ; Mudcat 57722 ; trad.]

Ewan MacColl sang Fourpence a Day on the 1955 Columbia anthology The World Library of Folk and Primitive Music: England, and on his 1957 10" Topic album Shuttle and Cage. This track was also included in 1965 on his extended 12" Topic album Steam Whistle Ballads, and on his anthologies The Definitive Collection (2003) and An Introduction to Ewan MacColl (2018). It also appeared in 1993 on Topicâs CD reissue of The Iron Muse. It was printed in Peggy Seegerâs and Ewan MacCollâs 1960 book on English and Scottish Folksongs, The Singing Island. The second Topic albumâs notes said:

Still current in North-East Yorkshire, this song is attributed to Thomas Raine, lead-miner and bard of Teesdale. The washing rakes, where the lead bearing rocks were separated from the clay and gravel, were usually operated by young boys or old disabled miners. The mine owners are said to have become so incensed by the song that they closed the pits and imported lead miners from Germany.

The song was collected by Joan Littlewood and Ewan MacColl from John Gowland, retired lead miner in Middleton-in-Teesdale, in 1948.

Jo Freya sang Fourpence a Day in 1992 on her Saydisc album Traditional Songs of England. The liner notes commented:

Young boys or disabled miners usually worked at the washing-rakes to separate the lead-bearing rocks from clay and gravel. The song is attributed to local Teasdale leadminer, Thomas Raine. Local legend has it that the mine-owners reacted to the song by temporarily closing the pits and importing leadminers from Germany.

Cross oâth Hands sang Fourpence a Day on their 2003 CD Saint Monday, and Doug Eunson and Sarah Matthews returned to it in 2006 on their album Proper Swell. They noted on the first albun:

Evidence of the proliferation of lead mining is clearly visible around the Derbyshire countryside, but the life was a hard one. Sarah learned Fourpence a Day at a Whitby Festival song swap and felt an instant affinity with the theme, although this song actually hails from Teesdale. With a new tune and time signature, the Derbyshire version was born.

Megson sang Fourpence a Day on their 2008 album Take Yourself a Wife, and live at Hitchin Folk Club, Hertfordshire, in November 2012 on their 2013 album Live.

Jackie Oates sang Four Pence a Day on her 2011 album Saturnine. She noted:

This set was put together by Karen [Tweed] and me during a tour-ette on the Isle of Wight. The song is one that I have been singing for a long time, and tells of the hardships of working on the washing-rake where the lead-bearing rocks were separated from the clay and gravel, usually operated by young boys or old disabled miners. The tunes that follow the song [The Oyster Girl and Off She Goes] are very well known English country dance tunes and are a chance for me to rejoice in being uncool! Thanks to Roger Wilson for remembering them to Karen.
