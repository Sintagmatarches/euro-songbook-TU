Helen of Kirkconnell Lea

[ Roud 8191 ; Ballad Index OBB152 ; DT HELNLIES ; Mudcat 56887 ; trad.]

David Herd: Ancient and Modern Scottish Songs, Heroic Ballads, etc., Second Volume Robert Chambers: Songs of Scotland Prior to Burns: Walter Scott: Minstrelsy of the Scottish Border.

Walter Scott published Fair Helen of Kirconnell in Volume 2 of Minstrelsy of the Scottish Border.

Robert Chambers noted in his 1862 book Songs of Scotland Prior to Burns:

In the burial ground of Kirkconnell, near the Border, is the grave of Helen Irving, recognised by tradition as Fair Helen of Kirkconnell, and who is supposed to have lived in the sixteenth century. It is also the grave of her lover, Adam Fleming—a name that once predominated the district. Helen, according to the narration of Pennant (PennantГўВҖВҷs Tour in Scotland, 1772), ГўВҖВңwas beloved by two gentlemen at the same time. The one vowed to sacrifice the successful rival to his resentment, and watched an opportunity while the happy pair were sitting on the banks of the Kirtle, that washes these grounds. Helen perceived the desperate lover on the opposite side, and fondly thinking to save her favourite, interposed; and, receiving the wound intended for her beloved, fell and expired in his arms. He instantly revenged her death; then fled into Spain, and served for some time against the Infidels: on his return, he visited the grave of his unfortunate mistress, stretched himself on it, and expiring on the spot, was interred by her side. A cross and a sword are engraved on the tombstone, with ‘Hic jacet Adamus Fleming’; the only memorial of this unhappy gentleman, except an ancient ballad which records the tragical event.

John Laurie recited Helen of Kirconnell in 1959 on the anthology The Jupiter Book of Ballads.

Fraser Bruce sang Helen of Kirkconnel on his 1974 album Fareweel tae Tarwathie. Drew Moyes noted:

“The tradition of the locality to the present day runs that Helen Irving, daughter of the laird of Kirkconnel in Annandale, about the latter end of the reign of James V was beloved by two gentlemen, Adam Fleming of Kirkpatrick whom she favoured, and another, said to have been Bell of Blacket House, who was favoured by her friends.” To cut a long story short Helen and Adam were out walking when they were confronted by Bell who shot at Fleming. Helen threw herself in front of her lover and was killed immediately. The song takes up the story.

Archie Fisher sang Helen of Kirkconnell Lea on a bonus track of the 1997 CD re-issue of his 1976 Folk-Legacy album The Man With a Rhyme. He noted:

The story which forms the subject of this ballad was first told in PennantГўВҖВҷs Tour in Scotland. It also appear in RitsonГўВҖВҷs Scottish Songs and in ScottГўВҖВҷs Minstrelsy. The tradition says that Helen Irving, daughter of the Laird of Kirkconnell in Annandale, at about the latter end of the reign of James V, was loved by two gentlemen, Adam Fleming of Kirkpatrick, whom she herself favoured, and another, said to have been Bell of Blacket House, who was encouraged by her friends. As Helen and Fleming walked on the banks of the Kirtle, her rejected suitor “leveled his carabine” at the breast of his rival. Helen threw herself before her lover, took the bullet in her own breast, and dropped, dying, in his arms. A thorn tree in the glen is pointed out to this day as the spot where she fell. The legend further tells us that Fleming avenged her immediately (“I hackit him in pieces sma’”) and later, upon returning from the wars in Spain, visited the lonely spot, and, overcome by grief, died on her grave.

The story [sung] here is somewhat abbreviated, which is probably just as well. (Information borrowed from Eyre-ToddГўВҖВҷs Ancient Scots Ballads, London and Glasgow, nd, by S.P.)

John Wright sang Helen of Kirkconnell in 2000 on his CD A Few Short Lines. He noted:

This is a romantic ballad that relates both to a specific locality and a specific event referred to in Sir John SinclairГўВҖВҷs Statistical Account of Scotland, published in the 1790s.

Helen Irving, daughter of the Laird of Kirkconnel in Annandale, was being courted by two men, Adam Fleming and Bell of Blacket House. The stage is set as Helen walks with her favoured lover, Adam Fleming, by the banks of the river Kirtle, unaware that his rival is lurking among the bushes on the opposite bank, at which point the story of the ballad is enacted. Following the tragic event, Adam Fleming left for the wars in Spain and on his return visited her grave whereupon, according to legend, “…stretching himself upon it, he expired, breathing her name with his last sigh…”—a barely credible though highly romantic note.

Their grave may still be seen in the churchyard of Kirkconnel near Springkell, with the inscription “Hie jacet Adamus Fleming” on the tombstone.

Fiona Hunter also sang Fair Helen of Kirconnell on the 2019 album ScottГўВҖВҷs Sangs that revisited the ballads of Walter ScottГўВҖВҷs Minstrelsy of the Scottish Border. Emily Lyle noted:

Scott believes that this ballad relates to true historical events in the 16th century involving two lovers named Helen Irving (or Bell) and Adam Fleming. As Scott concedes, the full details of the real story are unclear, but in the song, Helen is mistakenly shot by a jealous rival who had attempted to kill Fleming. Scott prints two parts to Fair Helen: the first was derived from a broadside and the second was current in oral tradition. Only the second part has survived to the present day in the repertoires of traditional singers.

Fiona Hunter first learned Fair Helen for a show with the Scottish author Andrew Greig based on his 2013 book of the same name, shortlisted for the Walter Scott Prize for Historical Fiction in 2014.

Fiona Hunter: “I think this is a beautiful but desperately sad ballad about love and loss and I am enjoying sharing it now with other singers and students.”

The spelling ‘Kirconnell’ is as given in the Minstrelsy; the modern spelling is ‘Kirkconnel’, the parish and town in Dumfries and Galloway.
