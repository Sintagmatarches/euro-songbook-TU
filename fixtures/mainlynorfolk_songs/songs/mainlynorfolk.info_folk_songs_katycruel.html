Kate Burke and Ruth Hazleton sang Katy Cruel on their 2015 CD Declaration. They noted:

Inspired by versions from both Karen Dalton and Linda Thompson, Katy Cruel is widely known as an American folk song but is believed to have Scottish origins and contains fragments of Irish traditional songs. It is said that the American version dates back to the colonial period and was sung by marching soldiers during the American War of Independence. First collected from Miss Lucy Allen and published by Eloise Linscott in Folk Songs of Old New England (1939), Miss Allen described Katy Cruel being sung: “As evening came, the children were put to bed upstairs, and around the great fireplace the men sang the haunting song with deep emotion.” Despite being an outcast, Katy is a wonderfully inspiring picture of a defiant and self-assured woman who refuses to conform to social expectations to be with the man she loves.

Josienne Clarke sang Katie Cruel, with two verses of her own, on a March 2026 download single. Her Bandcamp website noted:

A reimagining of a traditional folksong, long associated with themes of exile, rejection and endurance, Katie Cruel has appeared in many guises having travelled anonymously through centuries of oral tradition, shaped and reshaped as it passed from voice to voice. The result is an understated and robustly declarative song. Clarke芒聙聶s voice—long admired for its clarity and surely now deserving its own place in the English folk canon—carries the marks of experience: scarred, steady, and unmistakably that of a survivor.

Yet the narrative is of agency, one that makes peace with loss. Despite prior versions plaintively yearning for better circumstances, Clarke flips the mood in her redoubtable style, which she acknowledges may have hindered her career. “The industry loves an ing脙漏nue and I can芒聙聶t be that anymore by virtue of age and circumstance,” she explains. “My self-possession—and speaking out against the misogyny of the music industry, for instance—did not win me universal popularity. In that light, Katie Cruel is less a lament than a monument to positive fatalism: dignified and undramatic yet deeply assured. The accompanying video, directed by Alec Bowman_Clarke, mirrors this duality. Clarke appears as two figures in the same stark landscape on a cold stone promontory under grey, glowering skies. Buffeted by the wind, she is cloaked in a black Victorian-era mourning dress and a sheer, ghostly white veil. The imagery is a dialogue between past and present—the twin selves not opposites but coexisting—as reversed footage of waves retreating over rocks suggests time collapsing in on itself, memory and experience occupying the same ground.

“I芒聙聶ve always loved this song, but a personal version eluded me for years,” Clarke continues. “Since turning 40 and entering a new phase of my life and career I芒聙聶ve enjoyed rediscovering it.”

Released without confirmation of a wider project, Katie Cruel stands alone by design. It is a song about being cast aside, sung by an artist who has chosen her own path regardless of consequence. In continuing the active folk tradition, Clarke reminds us that songs endure because they can communicate uncomfortable truths—and they need voices brave enough to carry them forward.
