Lizzie Higgins sang MacCrimmonâs Lament in 1970 in Aberdeen to Peter Cooke and Ailie Munro. This recording was included in 2006 on her Musical Traditions anthology In Memory of Lizzie Higgins. Cha Till e Tuille—Cumha Mhic Criomain (No More Returning—MacCrimmonâs Lament) is a pibroch (complex variations on a theme set for the Highland bagpipe) said to have been composed in 1745 by Donald Ban (‘fair’ haired) MacCrimmon, hereditary piper to Norman, 19th chief of the MacLeods of Harris and Dunvegan, in premonition of his death before the battle of Culloden the following year. Unfortunately for romance, he and his chief were on the ‘wrong’ (i.e. Hanoverian Government) side and Donald was unlucky enough to be the only one killed in the ignominious ‘Rout of Moy’. This was an attempt by the Hanoverians to capture Prince Charles Edward Stuart (‘Bonny Prince Charlie’) quartered at the House of Moy near Inverness, in a midnight attack. The plans for this were discovered by Jacobite spies and the Hanoverians ambushed in a thunderstorm and routed in panic. MacCrimmonâs body was carted back to Inverness for burial. The original Gaelic verses (which themselves have evolved over time into variants) are attributed to Donaldâs sister, but lush English variant translations abound. (This pibroch, by the way, should not be confused with a later dedicatory pibroch composed by his brother, The Lament for Donald Ban MacCrimmon as sung and played on the marvellous R. Brown & R.B. Nicolâs Masters of Pibroch Vol 7, Greentrax CDTRAX279).

Isla St Clair has recorded it several times, most recently in The Lady and the Piper, Highland Classics HCLA-C103, where it is followed with its bagpipe tune played by Gordon Walker. As a young girl, Lizzie learned the words of this from a book, and her father taught her its pipe tune. She regarded it as an extremely difficult piece both to learn initially and perform, especially the ‘pipe decorations’.

According to legend, the MacCrimmon in question was Donald MacCrimmon, of the MacCrimmon family who, again legend has it, were the famous Skye family of pipers. He is supposed to have been killed at a small skirmish called the Rout of Moy in 1746 during the last Jacobite campaign.

Legend also has it that he had a premonition of his death and composed this tune the night before. The words are reputedly by his sister.

Isla St Clair sang MacCrimmonâs Lament with quite different verses on her 1972 Tangent album Isla St Clair Sings Traditional Scottish Songs and on her 1993 CD Inheritance. The latter version was also included in 1998 on the anthology Huntingdon Folk 2. Hamish Henderson noted on the first album:

The MacCrimmons, pipers to the MacLeods of Skye, were the most famous piping family in Scotland, and indeed in the Western world. Pupils came from far and wide to receive tuition at their college at Boreraig, near Dunvegan. Some of their great pibrochs—for example, Lament for the Children—are now accounted musical treasures by international critics and musicologists. The repression of Gaelic culture which followed the battle of Culloden seriously harmed but did not destroy their inheritance. Ironically, the MacLeods were on the Hanoverian side in the â45, and the piper who prophesied his own death before leaving for the field was killed in a skirmish against soldiers of Prince Charlie.

The translation usually sung was written by Professor John Stuart Blackie in the last century. The one Isla sings was made by Geordie Macdonald in 1966. Geordie is a native Gaelic speaker from Lewis.

Hamish Henderson describes this song as “a folksong variant of a 19th century translation from the Gaelic.” There is controversy as to why the poem was written, the translation I found reads:

MacCrimmon will never, will never return.
In war or in peace, he will come no more;
With riches or otherwise MacCrimmon will return not:
He will not come ever till the Gathering-day.

Hamish it was, too, who introduced me to the singing of Jeannie Robertson, from whose version this was learned. That fine singer died this spring and the most fitting tribute I can pay her is to cherish the tradition that she bore in such grand style.

Sam Lee sang McCrimmon on his 2024 album Songdreaming. He noted:

A simple elegy to the power of nature and a meditation on the concept of ‘awe’. This song takes the iconic tune McCrimmon composed in 1745, and later poetically adapted in the 19th Century into a lament with the cry of “No more, no more”. Here, however, Iâve turned tragic themes into a love song, a spiritual and essentially devotional hymn to nature. I knew this song first as one of the great ballads, sung to me by my teacher and legendary Scottish Traveller Stanley Robertson. He in turn inherited it from his aunt Jeannie who brought it to popular attention in the 1950s. Stanley often spoke of this song as one of his “thoosand calorie ballads” as it was so powerful it took a thousand calories to sing and described it as one of the hardest to deliver properly. Would I ever be ready to sing McCrimmon I often wondered, answering always with a ‘one day, maybe when I am older and wiser’. But quite unexpectedly the song offered itself as the basis for a new narrative, carrying in it that theme of transcendental love. In its metamorphosis into this new ballad, it now captures for me the quality of when the ordinary is transformed into magical and when nature does the wondermaking. Itâs sung as a reminder to look for the partings in that veil that separates the material and the magnificent. A pledge to never tire in your quest to find yourself in places where nature fully reveals her most glorious offerings of majesty and beauty.
