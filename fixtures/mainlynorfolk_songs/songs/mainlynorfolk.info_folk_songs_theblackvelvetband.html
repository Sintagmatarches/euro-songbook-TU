The Black Velvet Band

[ Roud 2146 ; Master title: The Black Velvet Band ; Ballad Index R672 ; Bodleian Roud 2146 ; Wiltshire 263 ; DT BLACKVEL , BLKVEL2 ; Mudcat 19899 , 51692 ; trad.]

The Singing Island

Harry Cox of Catfield, Norfolk, sang The Black Velvet Band to Ewan MacColl in 1955. This recording was included in 2000 on his Topic anthology The Bonny Labouring Boy. Steve Roud noted:

This is one of Harryâs songs which became popular in the post-war folk song revival, usually without acknowledgement. Surprisingly few versions have been noted—only half-a-dozen in England and Ireland but more in Australia. The subject-matter and the presence of cant words in some versions have led some to guess at a stage origin for the song, which is certainly possible. The few known broadsides printings point to an origin about 1840. The mention of Belfast in the first verse of most traditional versions has led some to presume an Irish origin, but the earlier versions invariably place the action in Barking, Essex.

Ewan MacColl sang Black Velvet Band in 1957 on his and A.L. Lloydâs Wattle EP Convicts and Currency Lads and on his and Peggy Seegerâs Riverside album Bad Lads and Hard Cases. He also sang The Black Velvet Band in 1960 on his Topic album Chorus From the Gallows, which recording was included in 2003 on his anthologies The Definitive Collection (2003) and An Introduction to Ewan MacColl (2018). He noted in 1960:

Harry Cox, the Norfolk folksinger, says this was a popular song with farm workers more than half a century ago. It has been collected as far away as Australia and, in the United States was a favourite hobo ballad—both as recitation and song. It still survives in England. Its history is rather obscure but it appears to have originated in the second half of the 19th century.

Cyril Poacher sang The Black Velvet Band to Tony Engle at home in Grove Farm, Blaxhall, Suffolk, on 8 August 1974. This recording was included in the following year on his Topic album of traditional songs from Suffolk, The Broomfield Wager, and in 1999 on his Musical Traditions anthology Plenty of Thyme. Rod Stradling noted in the accompanying booklet:

Henry Parker Such printed The Black Velvet Band on a broadside during the period 1869-86. His lengthy version, without chorus, is full of slang and cant terms which at times obscures the plot. The later music-hall no doubt added the chorus providing us in the process with a song which is still popular today. Cyril Poacher learnt the song in the 1950s from Alf Moseley of Harwich when the latter took his summer holidays in Blaxhall. As with The Faithful Sailor Boy, this common song has a few variations on the ‘standard’ melody—whether these are Cyrilâs or Alfâs is not known, but the result sounds, to me, like what Cyril seems to enjoy doing with a tune everyone knows.

It (well, Harry Coxâs version) is mentioned in The Singing Island where the editors quote Bert Lloydâs opinion that it probably began as a stage song in the 1890s. Roud reports 12 versions from tradition plus a variety of songbook and broadside sources. The song seems particularly common in Australia and New Zealand where it has provided the basis for an emigrant ballad in which the sweetheart, with her black velvet band, is forced to remain behind. There is a also a version published by Maureen Jolliffe in the Third Book of Irish Ballads, Mercier 1970, as The Black Ribbon Band, which sets the scene of the action in Tralee. This version was taken up by Irish showbands as a sort of cover of the Dubliners/Harry Cox one—apparently, it made the hit parade there.

Finally, there is a country music standard called The Girl in the Blue Velvet Band. This is not so much a version as a rewrite. Frank Shay prints an enormous text, about 33 verses, in More Pious Friends and Drunken Companions, 1928, and it gets a mention in Bluegrass Breakdown by Robert Cantwell [1992]. Neither publication names the author, but Cantwell says it is a Bill Monroe song. It may have come from the great Cliff Carlisle—thereâs a â30s record of Carlisle singing it (superbly).
