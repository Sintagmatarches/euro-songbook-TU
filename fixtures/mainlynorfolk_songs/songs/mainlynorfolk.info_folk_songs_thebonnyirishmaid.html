The Bonny/Lovely Irish Maid / Blackwaterside

[ Roud 6319 ; Ballad Index Pea551 ; DT BONIRSHM , BONIRSH2 ; Mudcat 979 ; trad.]

Louis Killen recorded The Lovely Irish Maid in winter 1977 at the Eldron Fennig Folk Museum of American Ephemera for his album Old Songs, Old Friends. He noted:

That period prior to 1961 also brought me that lovely air, Blackwaterside, from the piping of Francis McPeake, in 1958, and the following year, The Lovely Irish Maid from the singing of Michael Cronin, of Macroom in County Cork, Eire, but who was living in Camden Town, London, at that time.

Sheila Stewart sang this song as Blackwaterside to Doc Rowe in Blairgowrie, Perthshire on 15 October 1998. These recordings were published in 2000 on her Topic CD From the Heart of the Tradition.

Mick Ryan and Pete Harris sang The Bonnie Irish Maid in 2001 on their WildGoose CD The Long Road. They noted:

This is one of the many Irish songs of parting. It tells of a young man who sets off to seek his fortune in America. Pete learned this lovely song from the singing of Liam Murphy of the Irish band ‘Moher’.

Lynne Heraud and Pat Turner sang The Bonny Irish Maid in 2007 on their WildGoose CD September Days. They noted:

A traditional Irish song of the imminent parting of two lovers, due to emigration.

Geoff Lakeman sang Bonny Irish Maid on his 2016 album After All These Years. He noted:

Irish ballads come in many guises and with different titles and one can get “pixie-led” trying to unravel the origins. A similar song, with different lyrics is Blackwaterside, a location mentioned in my version. The song tells of a young man, about to set off for America, promising his lover that heâll stay faithful. She says sheâll wait for him.

George Duff sang The Bonnie Irish Maid on his 2018 album The Collier Laddie.

Sandra Joyce sang Blackwater Side on her 2023 album of songs of love and loss in the Irish tradition, Since You and I Have Been. She noted:

There are many songs with this name, and indeed a similar theme—the narrator is eavesdropping on two lovers in a rural idyll, and the man is about to emigrate. There are two Blackwater rivers in Ireland, one in Munster and one in Ulster. Given the origins of my learning of the song, I like to think that this is about the Ulster Blackwater, which flows through counties Armagh, Tyrone and Monaghan. I learned this from the singing of Peter Dundon, from Askeaton, Co. Limerick, one of the best and most unassuming singers I have ever known! Peter learned it from Rita Gallagher, the great traditional singer from Co.Donegal, who recorded it on her 1997 recording, Easter Snow.
