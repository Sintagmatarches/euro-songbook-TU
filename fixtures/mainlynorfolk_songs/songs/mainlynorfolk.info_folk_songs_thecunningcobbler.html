The Cunning Cobbler

[ Roud 174 / Song Subject MAS769 ; Master title: The Cunning Cobbler ; Ballad Index CoSB224 ; Bodleian Roud 174 ; trad.]

George Spicer of Copthorne, Sussex, sang The Cunning Cobbler on 4 February 1956 to Peter Kennedy. This BBC recording 23093 was also included on the anthology Songs of Seduction (The Folk Songs of Britain Volume 2; Caedmon 1961; Topic 1968). Another recording made by Brian Matthews on 12 November 1959 at The Oak Tree, Ardingley, was included in 2001 on the Musical Traditions anthology Just Another Saturday Night. Rod Stradling commented:

This looks like a fairly common song, having 53 Roud entries, but closer examination reveals that only 15 named singers provided these—the remainder being broadside examples. George learned it from Ike Harvey, landlord of The Rose, West Langdon, who “had the words on a broadsheet”. It appears to be known fairly widely in southern England, from Devon to Essex, with only Walter Pardon (Knapton, Norfolk) and William Short (Lincolnshire) being outside that area.

Mike Yates noted on the Topic album:

The Cunning Cobbler has all the characteristics of an ancient ballad. Admittedly the comic policeman is a late addition, possibly by Henry Such who was the last printer to issue it on a broadside, but the central theme is one which is well loved by Chaucer and Boccaccio. Other 19th century sheets were issued by Walker of Durham and John Sanderson of Edinburgh, and it is from these that the set collected in Essex in 1904 by Vaughan Williams together with the two versions noted in Hampshire in 1906 by J.F. Guyer originate. The present tune is an old favourite among folksingers and has seen service with a number of songs, the most notable being Turpin Hero.

Alex Bloomfield sang Cunning Cobbler to Keith Summers in between 1971 and 1977. This recording was published on the 2006 Veteran CD of traditional folk songs, Music Hall songs, and tunes from Suffolk recorded by Summers, Good Hearted Fellows. Mike Yates noted:

This forerunner of the Whitehall farces first appeared sometime around 1830 when a dozen or so broadside printers issued the song on their respective sheets. These included Birt, Catnach, Disley, Fortey, Hodge and Such, all of London; Walker and Ross, both of Newcastle; Forth of Hull; Wheeler of Manchester, Jackson of Birmingham; Harkness of Preston and Willey of Cheltenham. According to folklorist Gerald Porter there was more to the humble cobbler than might first have been suspected. “A shoemakerâs songs, like himself, can be simultaneously admired, copied and ridiculed and the significations of the occupation can be displaced, challenged or appropriated… Everyday order is challenged and opposites are mingled.” (Folk Music Journal, 1995).

Brian Peters sang The Cunning Cobbler in 1985 on his Fellside album Persistence of Memory. He noted:

An early piece of farce that might have made a good “Carry On” film, with Kenneth Connor in the title role, quaking under the bed, while Sid James and the others charged around dropping trousers and double entendres indiscriminately.

Tom Smith from Thorpe Morieux, Suffolk, sang The Cunning Cobbler in a recording made by John Howson in 1993. It was included in 2011 on the Veteran anthology CD of traditional folk music, songs and stories from England and Ireland, Stepping It Out Again!. John Howson noted:

This song is often entitled The Cunning Cobbler Done Over or The Cobbler and the Butcher. Ralph Vaughan Williams described it, when it was published in the EFDSS Journal in 1906, as “a modern example of the kind of rough fun which we find in Chaucerâs Clerk of Oxenforde”. The song was collected all over southern England by 20th century folk song collectors usually with very similar word sets. This is not surprising as it was widely published by the major 19th century broadside printers, including in London: Such, Fortey, Disley, Catnach and Hodges. What is unexpected is that it does not seem to have appeared in other parts of the country (with one exception in Lincolnshire) although many of the provincial printers in the north and the midlands also favoured the song.

Chris Foster sang The Cobbler and the Butcher in 2004 on his Tradition Bearers album Jewels. He noted:

Malcolm Austen gave me a set of words for The Cobbler and the Butcher as sung by George Spicer, a farm worker from Kent. I already somehow had the tune from somewhere. The song has a great story, worthy of Chaucer, somewhere between pantomime and farce. I changed the text around a bit, including the last verse, which had the cobbler going home and beating up his wife.
