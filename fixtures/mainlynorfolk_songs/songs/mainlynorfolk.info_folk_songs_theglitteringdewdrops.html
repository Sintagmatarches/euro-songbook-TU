Morning Stands on Tiptoe

[ Roud 24896 ; GlosTrad Tiptoe ; Wiltshire 1014 ; Mudcat 72345 ; trad.]

Alasdair Clayre: 100 Folk Songs and New Songs Alfred Williams: Folk Songs of the Upper Thames

Steve Roud distinguishes Morning Stands on Tiptoe (Roud 24896) and The Glittering Dewdrops. (Roud 24897) by their first lines but the recorded versions here freely share verses and chorus so they look to me like variants of the same song.

Dave and Toni Arthur sang Morning Stands on Tiptoe in 1967 as the title track of their Transatlantic album Morning Stands on Tiptoe. They noted:

The version sung here is a collated version using two verses from Alfred Williamsâ Folk Songs of the Upper Thames who collected the song from Charles Messenger at Carney Wick and the chorus, last verse and tune from the singing of George Townsend of Lewes, Sussex, who calls it Glittering Dewdrops. Alfred Williams considered it unsurpassed as a hunting song.

The Trugs sang The Echoing Horn on their 1971 Traditional Sound Recordings album And Boldly Go to Sea.

Gary and Vera Aspey sang Morning Stands on Tiptoe in a March or May 1976 live performance on their 1976 Topic album A Taste of Hotpot. They noted:

We finish as we began, with another hunting song. A favourite of ours which we enjoy singing. The reference to ‘puss’ in the last verse applies to any furry animal.

Alfred Williams felt this ‘unsurpassed as a hunting song’, but it turns into a love song in the last verse. It was noted a few times in Southern England, but seems to have been rather rare. Alison found that it was published in the Sporting Magazine in 1803 where it seems close to an Art song with the line “A nymph to pursue thatâs more bright than the day”. Both Alfred Williams and George Gardiner noted a much more folk-like version without any nymphs. It is open to debate which came first.

We first heard Dave and Toni Arthurâs rousing version of this old hunting song but we have taken the liberty of transforming it into a joyful ode to the breaking dawn and all that can be revealed and renewed through the promise of morning.
