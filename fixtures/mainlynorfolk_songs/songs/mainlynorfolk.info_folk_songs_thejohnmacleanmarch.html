The John MacLean March

[ Roud 42094 ; DT JHNMCLN ; Mudcat 7730 , 44552 ; words Hamish Henderson, music trad.]

The Clutha sang The John MacLean March on their 1971 Argo album Scotia!. Don Martin noted:

The memory of John MacLean is undoubtedly cherished more than that of any other Clydeside workers芒聙聶 leader. His ideas on education of the people in Marxist principles are now a legend. This song was written by Hamish Henderson of the School of Scottish Studies to a traditional pipe tune. It has been praised by Hugh MacDiarmid. the poet.

Dick Gaughan sang The John MacLean March in 1972 on his first album on the Trailer label No More Forever. A live recording from St Andrews Folk Club on 27 October 1974 and a video from the Cellar Upstairs Folk Club made by Dock Rowe in 1986 were included in 2025 on his box set R/evolution 1969/83. Another live recording from the Sunflower Folk Club on 30 April 1982 was included in 2025 on his CD Untroubled: Live in Belfast 1979-82. He noted on his album:

[John MacLean] was a Scottish schoolteacher and Marxist educator who was sentenced to two years芒聙聶 imprisonment in 1918 for agitating against the carnage of World War 1. Due to popular outrage and demonstrations, he was released after 7 months but the harsh treatment he received in prison seriously damaged his health and he died a few years later.

His vision of an independent Scottish Socialist Republic disagreed strongly with Lenin and with Gallacher and other leading Scots Communists of the time as he was opposed to the integration of the Scots within the British Communist Party.

Alex Campbell, Dick Gaughan, Finbar Furey, and John Faulkner sang The John MacLean March on the 1981 album Folk Friends 2 that was recorded at Hannes Wader芒聙聶s windmill ‘Fortuna’ in Struckum, Germany.

The Laggan sang The John MacLean March on the 2003 Hamish Henderson tribute album A芒聙聶 the Bairns o Adam.

Danny Spooner sang John MacLean芒聙聶s March on his 2008 CD Brave Bold Boys. He noted:

Written by Hamish Henderson to a pipe march of the same name the song honours a great fighter for human rights. In the early 20c, Clydeside芒聙聶s John MacLean, a teacher, was imprisoned and tortured for anti-war activities and trying to teach socialism in schools. He was elected to parliament while in prison. A master orator, he urged men not to fight, because “there is a worker on both ends of a bayonet”. He died as a result of his ill treatment in prison and it is said that one hundred thousand followed his funeral through Glasgow.

Ian Bruce sang The John MacLean March on his 2011 “first 30 years” anthology Hits & Pieces. He also sang it with his brother Fraser Bruce on their 2017 album Auld Hat New Heids.
