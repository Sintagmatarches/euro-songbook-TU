This excellent comic song has a long and rather complicated history. Stenhouse refers to a “humorous but vulgar ballad” of about 1700 about a young lady of rank who fell in love with one of her father芒聙聶s tenants and married him. This is presumably the song of which a fragment is preserved in Herd芒聙聶s Ancient and Modern Scottish Songs (1769, vol. 2:201). The first verse runs:

And shooling the grupe sae clean

In August 1793 Robert Burns wrote to George Thomson: “Another favourite air of mine is The Muckin芒聙聶 o芒聙聶 Geordie芒聙聶s Byre. When sung slow with expression, I have wished that it had better poetry—that I have endeavoured to supply.” The result was hardly one of his more felicitous efforts. There can be little doubt that the old “vulgar ballad” contains better poetry than Burns芒聙聶 sentimental ditty.

The version Jimmy McBeath sings here, which is widely regarded in the North East as ‘his’ version, is comparatively modern; it was made popular between the wars by the professional entertainer Willie Kemp, who sang it up and down the country at “bothy nicht” concerts, and recorded it on a Beltona 78 record. A slightly different version was put into currency by another popular ‘stage’ bothy singer, George Morris of Old Meldrum. Both these performers laid claim to the title held by Jimmy himself of “King of the Comkisters”. Their versions are probably descendants of an earlier song, maybe stemming from a relative of Herd芒聙聶s fragment.

The recording was made at a ceilidh in aid of War-Blinded in Linbum Village, August 1959.
