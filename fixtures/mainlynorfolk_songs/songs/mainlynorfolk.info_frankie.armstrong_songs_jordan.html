Jordan

[ Roud 2103 ; Ballad Index R305 , CSW188 ; VWML SBG/1/2/718 ; Jordan at Old Songs ; Folkinfo 150 ; DT JRDNHARD ; Mudcat 53122 , 20623 ; Daniel Decatur Emmett / trad. arr. Frankie Armstrong / trad. arr. Alison Burns]

According to Lyle Lofgren, Daniel Decatur Emmett (1815-1904) “took credit for the first version of Jordan Is a Hard Road to Travel, for an 1853 New York minstrel show. It was hugely popular and spread to the hinterlands via travelling shows. ThereГўВҖВҷs some evidence that he took an existing song and substituted political and topical verses. Later minstrel performers updated the commentary depending on what was in the news.“

and Frankie Armstrong added to this:

Bellowhead learnt Jordan from Frankie ArmstrongГўВҖВҷs album and recorded it in 2006 for their CD Burlesque; and Jon Boden sang it as the 24 August 2010 entry of his project A Folk Song a Day. They noted:

From the American white minstrel movement, this was written by Daniel Decatur Emmett who was also responsible for Dixie, Old Joe Clark and The Blue-Tailed Fly. Published in America in 1853, it travelled quickly to England where it was printed on a number of broadside ballad sheets which contain new references to the Indian Mutiny of 1857-1858. Sabine Baring-Gould, song collector and author of Onward Christian Soldiers, took two English oral versions from Thomas Darke and Sam Fone of Holcombe Burnell, Devon, in the 1880-90s.

These can be found in Sabine Baring-Gould, Garland of Country Song, London: Methuen, 1895, pp. 22-25, and Roy Palmer, Room for Company, Cambridge: CUP, 1972, pp. 24-35, both out of print. (…) The broadsides are in the Madden Collection in Cambridge and the Bodleian Library in Oxford.

Steve Turner sang Jordan in 2012 on his Tradition Bearers CD Rim of the Wheel. He noted:

Frankie Armstrong sang a version of this 19th century American protest song in 1971 on a compilation album of songs collected by Roy Palmer called Room for Company. This was also my first ever recording (I was featured on one song) when l was with the band Canny Fettle. After forgetting about it for 40 years I recently got an e-mail from my friend Ali Burns in Scotland telling me that sheГўВҖВҷd written a variation to the tune given to Baring-Gould by Sam Fone [VWML SBG/1/2/718] . I grabbed the opportunity to have a go at such a powerful setting.

The song has mysterious origins having been changed so much over the years. But Dan Emmett (1815-1904) a New York minstrel composer seems to have been the first person to use the song in a show in America in 1853. He took the existing song and substituted political and topical verses.

Jeff Warner sang Jordan is a Hard Road to Travel in 2018 on his WildGoose CD Roam the Country Through. He noted:

It was his re-working of Dan EmmettГўВҖВҷs song written in 1853—which very well might have been a parody of an earlier song. Emmett (1815-1904), along with his band the Virginia Minstrels, helped bring the minstrel craze to the New York stage in 1843. Uncle DaveГўВҖВҷs verses comment humorously on his times, and hint at his talent as a stage entertainer and banjoist. Macon recorded about 170 songs in his career, even though he didnГўВҖВҷt become a professional musician until he was in his 50s. He was known as the “Dixie Dewdrop”.
