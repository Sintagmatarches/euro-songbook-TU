On Christmas Night (The Sussex Carol)

[ Roud 597 ; Ballad Index KaCr108 ; VWML RVW2/2/102 ; Bodleian Roud 597 ; GlosTrad Roud 597 ; Folkinfo 564 , 597 ; DT SUSCAROL ; Mudcat 15795 ; trad.]

Maud Karpeles: Cecil SharpГўВҖВҷs Collection of English Folk Songs The Crystal Spring Steve Roud, Eddie Upton, Malcolm Taylor: Still Growing

On Christmas Night All Christians Sing was collected by Ralph Vaughan Williams from Mrs Verrall of MonkГўВҖВҷs Gate, Horsham in Sussex, on 24 May 1904 [VWML RVW2/2/102] . From this location it is also known as The Sussex Carol.

Tony Wales sang On Christmas Night in 1957 on his Folkways album Sussex Folk Songs and Ballads. Kenneth S. Goldstein noted:

The earliest known text for this carol appeared on a 19th century broadside, though it is possible that the carol was known in tradition from an earlier period. It has not been collected frequently from tradition; several versions have been found in Sussex, however. The version sung here (with one stanza omitted) was collected by Ralph Vaughan Williams from Mrs Verrall of MonkГўВҖВҷs Gate, Sussex. It appears to have been almost completely lost in tradition, though it is still sung during the Christmas season as learned from the many carol collections in which it is included.

Shirley Collins with the Etchingham Steam Band sang The Sussex Carol on 16 December 1974 at Cecil Sharp House in London. This recording was included in 1995 on their eponymous CD The Etchingham Steam Band.

Magpie Lane sang The Sussex Carol in 1995 on their Beautiful Jo CD Wassail! A Country Christmas, and Carol and Andy Turner sang it as the 24 December 2011 entry of AndyГўВҖВҷs project A Folk Song a Week. He commented in his blog:

Not, of course, specifically a Sussex carol: the Roud Index lists versions collected from oral tradition in counties including Shropshire, Gloucestershire, Surrey, Herefordshire and Hampshire; while the Bodleian has copies of early nineteenth century ballad sheets printed in London and Birmingham. Indeed, according to Wikipedia the words were first published by Luke Wadding, a 17th-century Irish bishop, in a work called Small Garland of Pious and Godly Songs (1684).

But Vaughan Williams collected this version from Mrs Verrall, of MonkГўВҖВҷs Gate, Horsham in Sussex, in 1904, and his arrangement of the carol was included in the Oxford Book of Carols, first published in 1928 (he had also incorporated it into his Fantasia on Christmas Carols, first performed in 1912).

IГўВҖВҷve always enjoyed singing this carol, but these days it has a particular significance for me as itГўВҖВҷs almost invariably the song which closes our Magpie Lane Christmas concerts in Oxford. As such it brings a mixture of emotion: exhilaration at the completion of a successful concert in front of a supportive home crowd, and a general feeling of goodwill-to-all-men-itГўВҖВҷs-almost-Christmas; mixed with a tinge of sadness because this yearГўВҖВҷs concerts are over, and we wonГўВҖВҷt be dusting off this repertoire again for another twelve months.

Here the lead vocals are taken by my wife Carol, with my eldest son Joe on fiddle. A very happy Christmas from all of us.

The New Scorpion Band sang The Sussex Carol in 2001 on their CD The Carnal and the Crane. They noted:

A mainstay of the modern Christmas choral repertoire, this carol would not be known to us if Ralph Vaughan Williams had not succeeded in meeting Mrs Harriet Verrall in the small Sussex village of MonkГўВҖВҷs Gate, near Horsham, in 1904. A splendid singer of traditional songs and ballads, Mrs Verrall was also the source for Our Captain Cried All Hands, which Vaughan Williams subsequently set as He Who Would Valiant Be. His choral arrangement of On Christmas Night all Christians Sing was published in Eight Traditional English Carols in 1919. The text is of late 17th century origin, the first version of it having been published in 1684, and it subsequently appears in the chapbook A Good Christmas Box in 1847.

Our arrangement is intended to evoke the village band, with brass instruments to the fore—concertina, cornet, euphonium, tuba, violin, percussion and vocals.

Cherish the Ladies sang On Christmas Night in 2004 as the title track of their Rounder album On Christmas Night.

Iontach sang the Sussex Carol on their 2008 Christmas album Flight of the Wren. They noted:

IrelandГўВҖВҷs County Wexford has a particular tradition of carolling which while sadly not as widespread as in former times, has not died out completely. Its origins are closely associated with two collections which were in the form of ‘garlands’. The first of these, the Smale Garland, was published in Ghent, France in 1684 and was compiled and probably written by Luke Waddinge who was the bishop of Ferns, County Wexford. On the title page the following verse or ‘posy’ is included.

The sweet and the sower
The nettle and the flower
The Thorne and the Rose
This Garland Compose

Published in the aftermath of severe Catholic repression in Ireland, it comprises of several religious verses and also contains eleven Christmas songs, some of which are still sung today. One such song is On Christmas Night All Christians Sing, and it is the earliest text for the carol now known as The Sussex Carol. The Smale Garland was reprinted in London, England, in 1728 and again in Drogheda, Ireland, in 1731. SiobhГғВЎn [Kennedy] first learned this carol when she was a member of the Marist (Saint MaryГўВҖВҷs) Church choir in her home town of Dundalk.

Kerfuffle sang the Sussex Carol in 2009 on their Midwinter album Lighten the Dark.

GreenMatthews sang The Sussex Carol on their 2011 CD A Victorian Christmas.

BelshazzarГўВҖВҷs Feast played Paul HutchinsonГўВҖВҷs tune Sussex Air and sang the Sussex Carol in 2012 on their Unearthed CD Stocking Fillers. They noted:

Opinions vary about the true origin of the Sussex Carol; some academics claim this was collected by Vaughan Williams but Paul H[utchinson] has it on good authority that it was the signature tune of the Little Hampton Male Voice Choir c.1930 and wrote something moving to go with it. Whereas Paul S[artin] vaguely recalls coming across it in the Book of Penguin Carols and claims that it was sung on 24—May 1904 to Vaughan Williams by Mrs Verrall at Monks Gate [VWML RVW2/2/102] , which is situated, aptly, in Sussex.

Vicki Swan and Jonny Dyer sang the The Sussex Carol on their 2014 album A Sound of Christmas Past. They noted:

A 17th century carol from Ireland and found in Small Garlands of Pious and Godly Songs. This version collected by Vaughan Williams.

A couple of our favourite well-known carol tunes that work well as jigs! We all hail from Sussex in one way or another, so it was important to get this one in!
