Carson Brothers & Sprinkle recorded The Old Miller芒聙聶s Will on 6 December 1929 in New York City for the 10" shellac record Okeh 49398. This recording was included in 1997 on the Yazoo CD of classic recordings of the 1920s and 30s of early American rural music, Times Ain芒聙聶t Like They Used to Be Vol. 1, and in 2015 on the Nehi anthology of British songs in the USA, My Bonnie Lies Over the Ocean on which Steve Roud noted:

Quite widely collected in England and Scotland, but even more popular in the USA and Canada, which goes to show that millers were proverbially regarded as rapacious and dishonest in the new world as well as the old. What is not so clear in the Carson Brothers version is that the old man questions his three sons in turn, and leaves the mill to the one who would take most ‘in toll‘. This ‘three sons’ pattern is a familiar motif in traditional tales and has led some commentators to assume a long history for the Old Miller song, but there is no evidence that it existed much before the 1760s.
