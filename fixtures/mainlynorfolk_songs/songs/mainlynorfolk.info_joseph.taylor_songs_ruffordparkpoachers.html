Rufford Park Poachers

[ Roud 1759 / Song Subject MAS708 ; Ballad Index OSLi13 ; VWML PG/5/21 , PG/6/7 , COL/2/39B ; trad.]

Joseph Taylor sang Rufford Park Poachers on Unto Brigg Fair, from a cylinder recorded in 1908. The LP sleeve notes said:

This version is unique and must be possibly the finest tune that Joseph Taylor sang. Mr Patrick O芒聙聶Shaughnessy had the good fortune to find a full account of this affray in the form of a poster and his notes to More Folk Songs From Lincolnshire should be consulted.

Nic Jones sang Rufford Park Poachers in a BBC Radio 1 John Peel Session recorded on 5 June and first broadcast on 11 July 1972. He never it an official album but live recordings from the late 1970s were included in 2001 on Unearthed, and in 2006 on his Topic album Game Set Match; the latter track was included in 2019 on his Topic anthology An Introduction to Nic Jones,

John Goodluck sang Rufford Park Poachers in 1976 on his Sweet Folk and Country album of traditional songs of Suffolk, Speed the Plough.

Another song of my ‘second choice’ nightly sport. A classic example of the struggle to stay alive by the poor man starving to death and watching the pheasants and hares being hunted for pleasure and protected by that enemy, the gamekeeper.

‘Rufford Park’ is in Nottinghamshire and the background of this particular battle seems somewhat obscure.

Mick Ryan and Jon Burge sang Rufford Park Poachers (with Dave Burland singing harmony) on their 1978 Leader Tradition album Fair Was the City.

Martin Carthy sang Rufford Park Poachers on his 1982 album Out of the Cut. A BBC live recording from July 1987 was published on Martin Carthy芒聙聶s Kershaw Sessions and reissued in 2001 on The Carthy Chronicles. He also sang it live in studio in July 2006 for the DVD Guitar Maestros. Martin Carthy noted on his original recording:

Rufford Park is not far from Mansfield, and in 1850 there was a showdown between local people and gamekeepers in the shape of a vicious and bitter fight, after which ringleaders were selected, tried and transported for up to 14 years. Patrick O芒聙聶Shaughnessy of the Lincolnshire Society did a reconstruction job on the words, three versions of which were recorded by Percy Grainger from Joseph Taylor, himself a retired gamekeeper, who was a teenager at the time of the events in the song.

Oyster Band sang Rufford Park Poachers on their 1983 album English Rock 芒聙聶n芒聙聶 Roll: The Early Years 1800-1850.

Home Service sang Rufford Park Poachers as part of Percy Grainger芒聙聶s Lincolnshire Posy suite on their 1986 Making Waves album Alright Jack.

Philadelphia group Broadside Electric sang Rufford Park Poachers on their 1994 album Amplificata. They noted:

We found this song on a Martin Carthy record. It chronicles a true event that took place in 1850 in northeastern England.

Coope Boyes & Simpson sang Rufford Park Poachers in 1996 on their No Masters album Falling Slowly. This track was also included in 1999 on their No Masters anthology What We Sing.

John Roberts and Tony Barrand sang Rufford Park Poachers in 1998 on their CD of English folksongs collected by Percy Grainger, Heartoutbursts. They noted:

This is another of the songs Joseph Taylor recorded for the Gramophone Company, though it was not issued. Grainger used it in Lincolnshire Posy as Rufford Park Poachers (Poaching Song). Indeed, it tells a dramatic tale of an event that took place in 1851, when Mr Taylor was a young man. A gang of thirty or forty poachers was attacked by ten gamekeepers, one of whom was mortally wounded during the battle. Four of the poachers were tried for his murder, found guilty of manslaughter, and sentenced to transportation for fourteen years. Mr Taylor remembered only three verses of the ballad, and Patrick O芒聙聶Shaughnessy, who discovered a broadsheet giving an account of the trial, completed the text.

Chris Foster sang Joseph Taylor芒聙聶s few verses of Rufford Park Poachers in 2004 on his Tradition Bearers album Jewels. He noted:

1n 1972 Bill Leader released the vinyl album Unto Brigg Fair. It was a fantastic selection of songs taken from wax cylinder recordings of traditional singers, made in 1908 by Percy Grainger in Lincolnshire. Rufford Park Poachers is one of several great performances on the album by Joseph Taylor, a bailiff on a big estate at Saxby-All-Saints. He was 75 at the time the recording was made, but his wonderful performance belies his years. Some of the songs that he recorded (but not this one) were released commercially by the ‘Gramophone Company’ on single sided 78rpm phonogram records where he was billed as a ‘Genuine Peasant Folksinger’.

Rufford Park in Nottinghamshire is now a craft centre. Times change.

Jim Moray sang Rufford Park Poachers in 2008 on his CD Low Culture. This track was also included on the 2009 fRoots compilation Looking for a New England.

James Yorkston sang Rufford Park Poachers on his 2009 CD Folk Songs.

Martin Simpson sang The Rufford Park Poachers in 2017 on his Topic album Trails & Tribulations. He noted:

The Rufford Park Poachers was one of the first traditional songs to be commercially recorded. The Australian song collector and composer, Percy Grainger, recorded the great Joseph Tailor, of Saxby All Saints, singing it in 1908. The story dates to 1950 when the local population of poachers rose up against the gamekeepers of the Dukeries Rufford Park near Mansfield in Nottinghamshire. Patrick O芒聙聶Shaughnessy, compiler of the excellent Yellowbelly Ballads collections, published by Lincolnshire and Humber Arts, put together this set of lyrics based on a broadside version of the song.

Friction and indeed violence between gamekeepers and the working poor in the countryside was commonplace after the Enclosure Acts. The Long Affray by Harry Hopkins is an excellent source of material on the subject of what became known as The Poaching Wars. Rufford Park is now a holiday attraction, although the high stone walls which surround it would still deter all but the most determined climber.

Ken Wilson sang Rufford Park Poachers on his 2018 CD Portraits. He noted:

From the singing of Joseph Taylor, the song tells us of a showdown in 1850 in Rufford Park near Mansfield, between local people and gamekeepers, in the shape of a vicious and bitter fight. After which, the ‘ringleaders’ were identified and transported for up to 14 years.

Ewan McLennan sang Rufford Park Poachers on his 2020 album Borrowed Songs and on the 2021 Landworkers芒聙聶 Alliance anthology Stand Up Now. He noted on his album:

The rights to common land and the struggles that took place in order to protect those rights are the subject of Rufford Park Poachers. The events in question took place around 1851 near Mansfield and are, I think, lyrically and sympathetically captured in this song.

Ruth Hazleton sang Rufford Park Poachers on her 2025 album Heronbones. She noted:

Another traditional song that appears to recount an historical event, Rufford Park Poachers was first recorded by Percy Grainger and sung by Joseph Taylor, a retired gamekeeper and teenager at the time of the event (1850). The tale tells the story of a violent clash between poachers and gamekeepers, arguably a consequence of the brutal Enclosure Acts instigated in England at the time, removing access to common lands and food sources from working class people.

Themes of class, wealth and resource inequality are timeless and universal and inspire this interpretation of the song. I learned this version from the singing of Nic Jones.
