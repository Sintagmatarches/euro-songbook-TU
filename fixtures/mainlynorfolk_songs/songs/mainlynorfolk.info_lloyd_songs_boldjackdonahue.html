Bold Jack Donahue

[ Roud 611 ; Laws L22 ; AFS 9 ; Ballad Index LL22 , MA063 ; Bodleian Roud 611 ; GlosTrad Roud 611 ; DT DONAHUE ; Mudcat 66885 ; trad.]

The earliest Donahue ballad appeared in The Sydney Gazette on 7 September 1830. On 2 January 1825 Jack Donahue arrived at Sydney Cove on board the convict ship Ann and Amelia. He had been sentenced to transportation for life on a charge “intent to commit felony.” He escaped and wrought on the planters and police until on 1 September 1830 Donahue, with his companions William Webber and John Warmsley, was ambushed by a party of police near Bringelly. Donahue was shot dead, while Webber and Walmsley escaped.

He was short, but a model of muscle and bone… He often said he was never designed for a prisoner and whilst he lived he would be free…” Twice he escaped from the iron gang, and the second time he and his band terrorised the Nepan countryside for a brief two years before he was trapped and shot by the police. The ballad must have been made by an admiring Irish convict shortly after Donahueâs death. It has a contempt for the law, a pride in the outlawâs independence, an appreciation of Donahue as the kind of man “who would fight till hell freezes over, and then write on the ice: Come on, you bastards!” It is this spirit which has kept the ballad of Jack Donahue going all these years since the troopers shot him in the Bringilly scrub on 1 September 1830. As good ballads will, Bold Jack Donahue exists in many versions. This one comes in the main from Bob Bell, rabbiter, of Condobolin, New South Wales, who has gone wherever the good-timers go.

Jack Donohue (often spelt Donahue) was born in Dublin in 1804. He supported the Irish nationalist cause and at the age of twenty was sentenced to transportation for life on a charge of ‘intent to commit felony’. On 2 January 1825 Jack Donohue arrived with two hundred others at Sydney Cove, New South Wales, on board the convict ship ‘Ann and Amelia’. Twice he escaped from the iron gang, and the second time he and his band terrorised both the planters and the police. In 1829, a reward of twenty pounds was offered for his capture. A year later it was increased to two hundred pounds. On 1 September 1830 he and his companions, William Webber and John Warmsley, were ambushed by a party of police near Bringelly. Donohue was shot dead, but the others escaped. He was seen by many as a hero and was often called ‘Bold Jack Donohue’.
