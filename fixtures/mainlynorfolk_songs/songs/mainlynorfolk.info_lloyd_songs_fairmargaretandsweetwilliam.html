A.L. Lloyd sang Fair Margaret and Sweet William in 1956 on his and Ewan MacCollâs Riverside album of Child ballads, The English and Scottish Popular Ballads Volume II. All of his ballads on this series were reissued in 2011 on his Fellside CD Bramble Briars and Beams of the Sun. Vic Gammon noted:

This ballad exists on seventeenth century broadsides and was anthologised in the eighteenth century. It is unusual among ‘Child” ballads in that its conspicuous early provenance is in English sources. He prints four texts, three English and one US version with a tune. This is, however, a widespread and popular ballad. Roud gives references to 324 printings and deposits; Bronson presents 79 tune versions and refers to two more in the addendum. As in most ballad totals, North American collected versions predominate.

In this version, after a beautiful but seemingly inconsequential first stanza, Margaret sees her lover with another woman and commits suicide by stabbing herself. She then returns as a ghost and discourses with her lover, although it is ambiguous whether he is awake or asleep. Margaret wishes him “joy of your gay bride bed”. In a superb ballad stanza, he then reports a dream to his bride:

“I dreamed a dream, my bride,” he said,
“Such dreams are never good.
I dreamed my bower was full of red swine
My bride bed full of blood.”

He calls his men and they go to Margaretâs bower but find her dead. He predicts his own death, dies and the ballad ends in the conventional rose and briar verses.

Lloyd has stuck closely to Mrs Thompsonâs version (as published by Kidson) in the earlier part of the song but departs from it after verse five, incorporating verses from other versions in Child. Mrs Thompson had a stanza which gives the ballad narrative coherence and makes it obvious that William has married someone other than Margaret:

Saying, “How do you like your soft feather bed?
How do you like your sleep?” (sic, sheet?)
“Very well like my soft feather-bed,
Very well I like my sleep
But much better I do like this pretty fair maid,
That lies in my arms asleep.”

We note the interesting intertextuality of this verse with Little Musgrave and Lord Barnard (Child  81, Roud  52). Lloyd obviously chose to shape his version with what he saw as the mainstream of the balladâs tradition. Lloyd sings the ballad making use of gapped phrasing with some rubato, mainly in the middle of his vocal range, edging into his higher register. The dispassionate and even tone Lloyd adopts, a sort of stoic strength that does not over-dramatise the tragic events of the ballad, is both a hallmark of good ballad singing and something which people coming to the ballad from other musical idioms often find strange.

Jean Ritchie sang Lady Margaret in 1956 on her Riverside album Saturday Night and Sunday Too. She sang it in 1960 with the title Sweet William and Lady Margaret on her Folkways album British Traditional Ballads in the Southern Mountains, Volume 2. Kenneth S. Goldstein noted:

This ballad traces back to at least the beginning of the 17th century, for two stanzas from it are quoted in Beaumont and Fletcherâs Knight of the Burning Pestle (ca. 1611). By the end of the 18th century, it had been printed frequently as a broadside or stall ballad, which may account for its popularity in tradition. Similarly, in America, where it has been collected frequently in versions quite far removed from the Child texts, frequent printings in early popular songsters may have accounted for its widespread distribution.

Like The Unquiet Grave, this ballad is a rich repository of popular superstitious beliefs: in it we find ghostly visitants, gruesome dream omens, the death kiss, and the continuity of love after death in the rose-and-briar motif.
