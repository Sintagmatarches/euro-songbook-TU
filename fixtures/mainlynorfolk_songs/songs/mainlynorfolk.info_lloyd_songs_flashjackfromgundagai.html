Flash Jack From Gundagai

[ Roud 24704 ; AFS 40 ; Ballad Index FaE146 ; Mudcat 172543 ; trad.]

A.L. Lloyd learned Flash Jack From Gundagai in Bethungra in the 1920s from a drunken shearer named White and recorded it in 1957 for the Wattle album The Banks of the Condamine and Other Bush Songs. He was accompanied by Peggy Seeger on guitar. Like all tracks of this album it was reissued in 1960 on the Topic LP Outback Ballads. Tt was also included in 1994 on the Larrikin CD The Old Bush Songs and in 2000 on his Fellside anthlogy Ten Thousand Miles Away. Lloyd noted on the Larrikin album:

What is special about Gundagai that earns it such frequent mention in folklore? Perhaps its position as a near-halfway stage on the Sydney - Melbourne highroad made it loom larger than life in the shearerâs imagination. Flash Jack evidently did most of his shearing a long way from home, but the fact that he came from Gundagai seems to give him a special character. He seems to be the elder brother of the Flash Stockman, but his boasts are more sober, and he is a workman of some dignity. The tune of this song is a close relative of the Bungaree melody.

If Crooked Mick [see Click Go the Shears] is a mythical figure, the real-life shearer is also heroic. In a single day on 10 October 1892, Jacky Howe shore 321 weaners at Alice Downs, Central Queensland. That was with the blade-shears. With the machine, Daniel Cooper shore 325 ewes at Langkoop, Victoria, on 22 October 1947. Shearers still think of themselves as aristocrats of labour, with vast pride in their skill. The song Flash Jack From Gundagai grew up in the slab huts of the shearersâ quarters, amid the smell of greasy wool, fried lamb-chops, shag tobacco and old newspapers. It gives the portrait of a dignified old workman whose boasts are just and sober.

David Lumsden sang Flash Jack From Gundagai on the 1963 EP Folk Songs of Australia and in a live recording from Melbourne Town Hall on the 1964 W&G album Australian Folk Night.

Martyn Wyndham-Read sang Flash Jack From Gundagai in 1966 on his W&G album Australian Songs and, accompanied by Nic Jones of fiddle, in 1978 on his German Autogram album Ballad Singer. He also sang Flash Jack From Gundagai and A Bush Lullaby in 1981 on his Fellside album Emu Plains which was also included in 1996 on his compilation CD Undiscovered Australia. Paul Adams noted:

Artfully—and quite feasibly—the songwriter splices into the lullaby a recurring theme from Flash Jack From Gundagai. Equally skilfully Martyn has combined the two songs.
