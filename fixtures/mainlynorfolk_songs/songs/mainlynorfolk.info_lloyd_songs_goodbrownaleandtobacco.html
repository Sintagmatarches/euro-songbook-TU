Good Brown Ale and Tobacco / All for Me Grog / Across the Western Ocean/Plains

[ Roud 475 ; Master title: Good Brown Ale and Tobacco ; G/D 3:580 ; AFS 2 ; Ballad Index K274 ; VWML GG/1/9/496 ; GlosTrad Roud 475 ; Wiltshire 542 , 809 ; DT HEREGROG , HEREGRO2 ; Mudcat 5512 ; trad.]

A.L. Lloyd sang All for Me Grog in 1956 on his Riverside album English Drinking Songs (and in 1961 on the Topic EP All for Me Grog which is just an extract of the LP). He commented in the albumГўВҖВҷs sleeve notes:

Jack Tar is no longer jolly—his boots are scuffed, the rags of his shirt-tail flog him in the breeze, the alcoholic horrors are not far off and itГўВҖВҷs time to look for a ship again.

An Australian variant of this, Across the Western Plains, is the title track of LloydГўВҖВҷs 1958 album, Across the Western Plains. He commented in the sleeve notes:

In the latter days of sail the seamen had a song called Noggin Boots or Across the Western Ocean. It was a bitter song about the sailor with his boots scuffed and his shirt-tail “looking out for better weather,” who has spent all his money on girls and gin, and now with the horrors not far off he has to take a ship across the Western Ocean once again. The song drifted to Australia, and some ingenious bushwhackers remade it to suit local conditions, and called it Across the Western Plains. The Sydney Bulletin printed a version of the Australian re-make in May 1916, at it was included in the 1926 edition of PatersonГўВҖВҷs Old Bush Songs.. No doubt, many country workers learned it from these two sources. The tune, well-known among seafarers, was not hard to come by. Sung straight, the song never seemed to me wildly exciting, but I once heard a drunken shearer named White sing it on a station near Bethungra, N.S.W., in a way that would make the hair stand on end. Ever since, IГўВҖВҷve tried to release this song from its hobbles, but IГўВҖВҷve never been as successful as Mr White.

Martyn Wyndham-Read sang Oh for Me Grog in 1966 on his album Australian Songs and in 1971 on his eponymous Trailer album Martyn Wyndham-Read.

The Watersons sang quite a different version of All for Me Grog in 1966 on their eponymous album The Watersons and on the Topic Sampler Sea Songs and Shanties. Like all but one track from The Watersons, it was re-released in 1994 on the CD Early Days. Once again, A.L. Lloyd commented in the original albumГўВҖВҷs sleeve notes:

Once again, the Watersons got this song from the collections of Frank Kidson. Helen Creighton, the distinguished Canadian collector, found a version in Nova Scotia and suggests that the song was originally a music hall favourite. As Across the Western Plains, the song is well-known in Australia, in versions relating to the adventures of a pastoral worker. As a sea shanty, the song was used in English ships for both capstan and halyard work.

Lea Nicholson sang this song as All Through the Beer in 1971 on his Trailer album Horsemusic.

Roy Harris sang All Through the Ale in 1972 on his Topic album The Bitter and the Sweet.

Then 90-years old George ‘Tom’ Newman of Clanfield, Oxfordshire, sang All for the Grog at home on 5 August 1972 to Mike Yates. This recording was included in 1975 on the Topic album of countryside songs from Southern England, When SheepshearingГўВҖВҷs Done, and, as My Old Hat That I Got On, in 1998 on the Topic anthology They Ordered Their Pints of Beer and Bottles of Sherry (The Voice of the People Series Volume 13). Mike Yates commented in the first albumГўВҖВҷs liner notes:

Until recently it was a custom in southern English pubs to perform All for the Grog as an ‘action’ song: the singer removing his clothes as the song progressed! The collector Alfred Williams did not appear to have witnessed such a performance, when, in 1923, he wrote, “An old song, though not a particularly inspiring one. Nevertheless, in spite of the poetГўВҖВҷs poverty, he could be optimistic, though I fear such optimism was rather feigned than real.”

Dave Williams sang All Through the Beer in a concert at Pimperne Village Hall, Dorset, in c.1975. This recording was included in 2003 on his posthumous Forest Tracks anthology YouГўВҖВҷre On Nipper!. Paul Marsh noted:

This song was collected by Dr George Gardiner from Charles Chivers of Basingstoke in 1906 [VWML GG/1/9/496] . Dave obviously enjoyed singing it and its length could be easily varied. He sings just two verses here.

Louis Killen, Jeff and Gerret Warner and Dud Benson sang All for Me Grog in 1976 on the Collector album Steady As She Goes. This track was also included in 2004 on the Smithsonian Folkways anthology Classic Maritime Music. Louis Killen also sang it in 1977 at the Philadelphia Folk Festival and in 2003 on the Fellside anthology Song Links: A Celebration of English Traditional Songs and Their Australian Variants. Dave de Hugard sang the corresponding Australian variant, Across the Western Plains.

Cyril Tawney sang All Through the Beer on his 1994 Neptune cassette of songs about drink and drinkers, Down the Hatch. It was included as All Through the Grog on his 2007 anthology The Song Goes On.

Walter Pardon sang HereГўВҖВҷs to the Grog in a recording made by Mike Yates that was included in 2000 on his Musical Traditions anthology Put a Bit of Powder on It, Father. Mike Yates commented in the accompanying booklet:

This song is almost only found in England—scattered the length and breadth of the country—aside from three examples from Australia and one each from Canada and Scotland. Only George ‘Tom’ Newman of Clanfield, Oxfordshire, and George Collinson of Hull, Yorkshire, are known to have recorded it apart from Walter.

Magpie Lane with Ian Giles in lead sang My Old Hat That I Got On in 2002 on their CD Six for Gold. They commented in their liner notes:

From Tom Newman of Clanfield in Oxfordshire. Born in 1882, Tom seems to have been quite a character. He played drums in a local dance band, and in later life often took his one-man band to Bampton on Whit Monday. John Baldwin, in the 1969 Folk Music Journal, wrote that he “tends to become very excited when singing: sitting in a chair and pumping the floor with his feet alternately, and similarly his knees with clenched fists”.

The All for Me Grog chorus turns up with various sets of words all over England, and as far afield as Australia. It was sometimes used as part of a MummersГўВҖВҷ play, as for example at Sapperton in Gloucestershire. Mike YatesГўВҖВҷs recording of Tom Newman singing the song can be heard on Volume 13 of the Voice of the People set.

We have been unable to find a definition of ‘gone for a sutter’. The meaning is obvious but what is a sutter? John Baldwin suggests it is a corruption of ‘asunder’; if you know better, do please let us know!

Jon Loomes sang All for Me Grog in 2005 on his Fellside CD Fearful Symmetry.

Danny Spooner sang Oh! For Me Grog live in June 2007 in concerts with the Australian Chamber Orchestra. A recording of these concerts was released in 2017 on his download album Enchanted: Live in Concert.

Jon Boden sang the WatersonsГўВҖВҷ version of All for Me Grog as the 21 October 2010 entry of his project A Folk Song a Day.

Belinda OГўВҖВҷHooley and Heidi Tidow sang All for Me Grog in 2015 on their CD SummatГўВҖВҷs BrewinГўВҖВҷ. They commented:

Traditionally a sailorГўВҖВҷs song which we heard sung by Louisa Killen. The chorus and first verse are traditional, and weГўВҖВҷve written the other verses to include some more, err, modern references!

GrannyГўВҖВҷs Attic sang All for Me Grog on their 2014 CD Better Weather, taking the albumГўВҖВҷs title from a phrase in this song. They commented in their liner notes:

This is a tradition song from the perspective of the sailor who, having spent all of his money on women and alcohol, must once more take a ship across the Western (Atlantic) Ocean. Our version is based on the one documented in Yorkshire in 1929 by Frank Kidson and Alfred Edward Moffat.

Jon Wilks sang My Old Hat That I Got On on his 2017 album Songs From the Attic.

The Exmouth Shanty Man sang All for Me Grog in 2022 on their WildGoose album Tall Ships and Tavern Tales. They noted:

Vice Admiral Sir Edward Vernon was nicknamed Old Grog because of an impressive grogam cloak he wore. In 1740 he issued an order, to curb the drunkenness on board ships, that rum rations be diluted with water. The song was collected from a Yorkshire sailor and published in 1929.
