Paul Clayton sang Sally Brown in 1956 on his Tradition album Whaling and Sailing Songs From the Days of Moby Dick. He noted:

This was a favorite shanty for heaving up the anchor. It dates at least to the early 1830s and was sung at the capstan of the Western Ocean packet ship that brought Captain Marryat from England to America in 1837, as he tells in his A Dairy in America (1839).

Mr Robbins had it as a halyard shanty, two pulls to each refrain, but most collectors give it as a capstan song. Stan Hugill speaks of ‘many obscene verse’ dealing with Miss Brown, the bright half-caste. Possibly the capstan versions are dirtier than the hauling versions, because of the dreariness of the job. Some versions have a very West Indian (presumed Jamaican) sound; others, like our version, less so. In the refrain, ‘roll’ is to be understood in the sense of ‘work steady’.
