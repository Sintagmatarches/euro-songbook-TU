The Banks of Green Willow is a song printed in Ralph Vaughan Williams芒聙聶 and A.L. Lloyd芒聙聶s The Penguin Book of English Folk Songs. It is related to Child 24 Bonnie Annie.

Cynthia Gooding sang The Banks of Green Willow in 1953 on her Elektra album of early English folksongs, Queen of Hearts. She noted:

Some ballads besides getting mixed with others, gradually lose their stories and become mere fragments of their former selves. Child prints a ballad called Bonnie Annie (No. 24), a long story song, of which The Banks of Green Willow is all that has been passed in tradition to the singer from whom this version was collected in 1906. The full story tells of a young man pressed to sea whose sweetheart accompanies him disguised as a man. On board ship after she calls for women to assist her in giving birth to her baby, the sailors decide the ship is jinxed. Lots are cast and each time it falls to her and her baby to be thrown overboard. This is done and when the ship comes into harbour, her body is found floating there. She is buried by her lover in a coffin variously of gold, or with gold nails. There is a song called The Undutiful Daughter which tells the same story and versions of the Daemon Lover (Child No. 243D) contain parallel verses.

A.L. Lloyd sang The Banks of Green Willow in 1956 on the Riverside anthology The English and Scottish Popular Ballads, Volume IV. He noted:

Child printed two Scottish texts of this ballad with its Jonah-like tale, and Gavin Greig reported two fragmentary texts, but the ballad is nevertheless a rarity in Scotland. It was apparently well known in England, however, for Sharp collected nine versions and reported it “very generally sung throughout Somerset”. Baring-Gould noted three versions in Devon, and J.F. Guyer recorded a Hampshire version.

All the English versions tell the story of a girl who elopes (aboard ship) with her lover and is thrown into the sea when the ship begins to founder. It is necessary to turn to the Child “A” text (from Kinloch芒聙聶s Ancient Scottish Ballads, 1827) to discover the reason for this action. There we find that everyone on board ship has cast lots to determine which of the passengers is guilty of some heinous crime, and the lot repeatedly falls on Annie. This derives from the sea-superstition that a vessel becomes unmanageable if a criminal is on board, and will not sail until the offender is removed; to discover this person, the casting of lots is usually resorted to. The same belief is an essential element in Brown Robyn芒聙聶s Confession (57).

The ballad is unreported from tradition in America, though various ballads (mainly from a broadside tradition) based on the belief discussed above have been collected in the New World. Included among these are Captain Glen, The New York Trader, and The Sailor芒聙聶s Tragedy.

The version of Bonnie Annie sung by Lloyd was collected by Cecil Sharp in 1903 from Lucy White and Louie Hooper of Hambridge, Somerset.

Like all of Lloyd芒聙聶s songs from this series this song was reissued in 2011 on his Fellside CD Bramble Briars and Beams of the Sun on which Vic Gammon noted:

Lloyd, following Child, was clear that the ‘Jonah’ motive, the expulsion of a wrongdoer to free an immobilised ship, explained the plot of this ballad. The pairing down of ballads into tightly economical forms sometimes involves loss of clarity but also a gain in intensity. The form of the song is contracted into just five verses, stark and understated, omitting almost all reference to motives for the action, just ‘he was troubled with her and her baby’.

Roud gives references to 90 published versions of the song and Bronson presents 18 variants, Child prints just three versions, two Scottish and one collected by Baring-Gould in Devon. Collected versions are mainly from the south and south-west of England. Considering the interest of the song, it has attracted relatively little scholarly attention.

Lloyd芒聙聶s performance of this tragic ballad is very restrained and intimate with plenty of ‘free air’ in the singing. This wonderfully economical version is from two sisters, Lucy White and Louie Hooper, who were among Cecil Sharp芒聙聶s best informants. Lloyd sticks closely to the text and melody as given adding only characteristic embellishments and rubato to the melody.

Sailors have long believed that a dead body, a murderer, or an accused person aboard ship will imperil its passage. In one older Scottish version, it is clear that this belief once was rooted in the fear of evil spirits and ghosts. The crew casts lots to find the Jonah:

The present fragmentary version was recorded from the singing of Mrs Maguire in Ayr on the West coast of Scotland. It has turned up in America only once, so far as we know, in a Maine text published by Phillip Barry.

Ewan MacColl sang the Scottish version Bonnie Annie in 1960 on his and Peggy Seeger芒聙聶s Folkways album Popular Scottish Songs. He noted:

Jonah ballads have never had the wide currency in Scotland that they have in England. Gavin Grieg was able to report only two fragmentary Scottish texts of Bonnie Annie against the 9 versions collected by Cecil Sharp in England.

The Jonah ballads are generally built round a central character who has taken ship in an attempt to fly from the consequences of it. In Child芒聙聶s “A” text of this ballad, the passengers on board a ship cast lots to determine which of them is guilty of a crime, and the lot falls repeatedly on Annie. The details of her “crime” are not given and, indeed, it is possible that her crime lies in the simple fact that she is a woman, since an old sea superstition holds that women on board inevitably bring bad luck at sea.

This belief is still widely held by fishermen in Great Britain today.

Brian Peters sang The Banks of Green Willow in 2008 on his album of Child ballads, Songs of Trial and Triumph. He noted:

Is her seducer actually the ship芒聙聶s captain, or a passenger? Why (in 24B) does he suddenly change his mind and send a lifeboat out after her? Most contain no mention of the captain芒聙聶s inability to sail the ship, leaving the motivation for jettisoning mother and baby a mystery. The intriguing exception is the version collected by Baring-Gould from James Masters of Bradstone, which includes the line “the sails were outspread but of miles made not any”. Here, it seems, we have evidence for a becalmed ship. Unfortunately Baring-Gould tinkered considerably with the texts he collected, deliberately inserting Child芒聙聶s “black bullets” stanza in one of his notated texts “to complete the story”, so it芒聙聶s difficult to be certain of the authenticity of his “sails outspread” line.

Many folk revival singers have covered the ballad, generally using the text and tune collected from Emma Overd by Cecil Sharp [VWML CJS2/10/346] as their starting point—although Frankie Armstrong芒聙聶s rendition of David Clements芒聙聶 version [VWML GG/1/9/535] (recorded by Vaughan Williams and available on the EFDSS CD A Century of Song) is a powerful alternative. My text is from several English versions, including snippets from the Baring-Gould text. The last verse seems to be unique to a version collected by George Gardiner from William Bone—I thought it rounded off the tale well.
