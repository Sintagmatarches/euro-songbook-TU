The Coast of Peru

[ Roud 1997 ; Laws D26 ; Ballad Index LD26 ; DT CSTPERU , CSTPERU2 ; Mudcat 132032 ; trad.]

Joanna C. Colcord: Songs of American Sailormen Alan Helsdon: Vaughan Williams in Norfolk

The Coast of Peru is a song about the early 19th century Pacific whale fishing.

A.L. Lloyd sang The Coast of Peru in 1954/5 on his, Ewan MacCollГўВҖВҷs and Harry H. CorbettГўВҖВҷs Topic album The Singing Sailor. This track has been reissued lots of times, e.g. on their albums The Black Ball Line (Topic, 1957), Shanties and FoГўВҖВҷcГўВҖВҷsle Songs (Wattle Records, Australia, 1957), Haul on the BowlinГўВҖВҷ (Stinson Records, USA, 1958), as title track of the EP The Coast of Peru (Topic, 1963), on the compilations Sea Songs and Shanties (Topic Sampler No 7, 1971), Chants de Marins IV: Ballads, Complaintes et Shanties des Matelots Anglais (Le Chasse-MarГғВ©e, France, 1984), and SailorsГўВҖВҷ Songs & Sea Shanties (Highpoint, 2004), and on his anthology Ten Thousand Miles Away (Fellside, 2008). He noted on the 1963 EP:

The English whaling ship Emilia was the first to inaugurate the Pacific sperm whale fishery in 1788, rounding Cape Horn to fish in the waters of the South Sea islands and the coasts of Chile and Peru. By the 1840s, the days of the South Seamen were numbered, but they left behind a fine memorial in their songs, of which The Coast of Peru is perhaps the most impressive. Tumbez, mentioned in the last verse, is in the far north of Peru, on the Gulf of Guayaquil, near the equator. Its girls are remembered in several whaling songs.

A.L. Lloyd also sang this song on his and Ewan MacCollГўВҖВҷs 1957 Riverside LP Thar She Blows!. He recorded The Coast of Peru for a third time in 1967 for the Topic album of ballads and songs of the whaling trade, Leviathan!. Here, he was accompanied by Alf Edwards, English concertina; Dave Swarbrick, fiddle; and Martin Carthy, mandolin, and he noted:

By no means all the oldtime whaling was done in northern waters. In the 1820s, for example, more than a hundred British ships, mostly out of Hull or London, where fishing in the spermwhale grounds round the Horn off the coast of Chile and Peru and taking the long, long run across the Pacific by way of Galapagos Island and the Marquesas, to Timor. The trip would last three years. The Coast of Peru is the most important ballad of the South-Seamen. Possibly it describes the chase of a southern right whale, not a sperm. Sperms were usually harpooned by running the boat close to the whale. Right whales, who tend to fight with their tail, were more often harpooned with the “long dart” from perhaps ten yards away. Mention of the mate in the “main chains” dates the song before the 1840s.

Ewan MacColl sang The Coast of Peru in 1963 on the B-side of the musical score from the film Whaler Out of New Bedford, where he was accompanied on banjo. According to the albumГўВҖВҷs notes the song was “collated from a text in Joanna C. ColcordГўВҖВҷs Songs of American Sailormen and from another in William DoerflingerГўВҖВҷs Shantymen and Shantyboys”.

Danny Spooner sang The Coast of Peru in 1966 on the Australian Score album A Wench, a Whale and a Pint of Good Ale and he sang The Coast of Peru in 2006 on his CD of songs of the whaling industry, The Great Leviathan. He noted on the CD:

After 1789 many British and American ships were rounding Cape Horn to hunt for the Sperm Whale. A vivid description of a whale chase, this song reminds us of the dangers of the chase and flensing at sea. ItГўВҖВҷs important to note the respect these men had for the ability of the whale to do them damage. Tumbes-Tonbas on a bay near the mouth of the Guayaquil River in Ecuador was where Admiral Parker took his fleet of British whale ships after a South Pacific raid during the 1812 war with America.

The Ian Campbell Folk Group sang The Coast of Peru in 1969 on their Music for Pleasure album Ian Campbell and the Ian Campbell Folk Group With Dave Swarbrick. They tersely noted:

A stark song about an everyday incident in a bloody and wasteful trade.

Lea Nicholson sang The Coast of Peru in 1971 on his Trailer album Horse Music.

Peter Marston sang The Coast of Peru in 1972 on the Living Folk, Cambridge, Massachusetts, anthology of traditional folk songs and ballads, Pleasant and Delightful Vol. 2. And David Jones sang The Coast of Peru in 1976 on the Living Folk album HereГўВҖВҷs a Health to the Man and the Maid.

Gerret Warner sang The Coast of Peru in 1976 on the Collector album of songs and chanteys from the days of commercial sail, Steady As She Goes. The album notes said:

Chris Foster sang The Coast of Peru in 1977 on his Topic album Layers and in 1999 on his Tradition Bearers album Traces. He noted:

A graphic description of a life and death confrontation between a group of men and a sperm whale. I learnt this from Bert Lloyd.

Crows sang The Coast of Peru in 1981 on their eponymous DingleГўВҖВҷs album Crows. This track was also included in 2016 on their WildGoose anthology Time to Rise. They noted:

Keith Kendrick sang The Coast of Peru in 2006 on his WildGoose album Songs From the Derbyshire Coast. He noted:

Another duet this time with my famous old sparring partner Lynne Heraud from the singing of A.L. Lloyd. A truly great song—and no—I do not support Whale hunting…

Hughie Jones sang The Coast of Peru in 2014 on his Fellside album Maritime Miscellany. He noted:

IГўВҖВҷve been a fan of Gregory Peck ever since I first watched Moby Dick. Since then IГўВҖВҷve seen the film dozens of times and have been lucky enough to stand in the boat shaped pulpit in the WhalemenГўВҖВҷs Chapel in New Bedford. In recent years IГўВҖВҷve been performing a lot in the USA, notably Mystic Seaport, Connecticut. Visitors there will find the New Bedford whaler Charles W. Morgan of 1841. The only ‘made up’ bit in my song A-Whaling on the Morgan is the whale man turned inn keeper Gordon Jordan. The rest of the story is absolutely true, Jordan is only there because it rhymes with Morgan.

Without doubt the best whaling song I know has to be The Coast of Peru. It tells in detail how hunting the sperm whale was conducted on the Pacific grounds which were discovered in the late 18th century. My version seems to be an amalgam from the following publications:-

Songs the Whalemen Sang by Gale Huntington
Chantying Aboard American Ships by Frederick Pease Harlow
Songs of the Sailor and Lumberman by William Doerflinger
Songs of American Sailormen by Joanna C Colcord

Kings of the South Seas recorded Coast of Peru at Cecil Sharp House, London, in June 2014 and released it in the same year on their eponymous first album, Kings of the South Seas.

Arthur Knevett sang Coast of Peru on his 2016 CD Simply Traditional. He noted:

A whaling song which graphically describes the hunt and killing of a sperm whale. Gale Huntington (Songs the Whalemen Sang) dates the song to the last quarter of the eighteenth century. This is corroborated by Gordon Jackson (The British Whaling Trade) when he tells us that the southern whale fishery, which includes the coast of Peru, underwent major expansion in the 1790s. I learnt this version from the singing of Bert Lloyd.
