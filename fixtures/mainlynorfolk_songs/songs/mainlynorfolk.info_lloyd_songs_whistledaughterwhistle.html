Peggy Seeger sang Whistle, Daughter, Whistle on her 1954 Signet and 1955 Folkways album Folk Songs of Courting and Complaint. She noted:

The theme of Whistle, Daughter is commonly found in Western Europe as well as throughout the United States. This particular version seems to be a more literary one, making use as it does of such abstract terms as ‘ingularity’, ‘propriety’, and ‘virginity’—words not typical of folk song and indicativc of urban influence.

A.L. Lloyd sang Daughter, Daughter, Whistle in 1956 on his Tradition album The Foggy Dew and Other Traditional English Love Songs. He noted:

Tom Lenihan sang a variant called Sixteen Years, Mama at home in Knockbrack, Miltown Malbay, Co. Clare, on 30 September 1977. This recording made by Pat Mackenzie and Jim Carroll was included in 1998 on the Topic anthology As Me and My Love Sat Courting (The Voice of the People Series Volume 15).

Mikey Kelleher from Quilty sang Daughter, Dearest Daughter to Jim Carroll and Pat Mackenzie in 1977. This recording was included in 2004 on the Musical Traditions anthlogy of songs and a recitation from the Carroll and Mackenzie collection, Around the Hills of Clare. They noted:

William Wells Newell described it as being identical to a German, Flemish and French round of the fifteenth or sixteenth century, in which a nun or monk is tempted to dance by various offers.

The earliest reference in English appears to be in a manuscript from Wiltshire dated 1740, though it seems to have survived longest in Ireland. We have recorded it on three occasions, from Mikey and Tom Lenihan, both of West Clare, and from Tipperary Traveller, Mary Delaney. Pat Mackenzie and Jim Carroll also recorded Mary Delaney singing Fourteen Last Sunday in between 1973 and 1985. This recording was included in 1986 on the VWML cassette of songs and a story of Irish Travellers, Early in the Month of Spring, and in 2003 on the Musical Traditions anthology of Irish Travellers in England, From Puck to Appleby. Kate Burke and Ruth Hazleton sang Eighteen Years Old on their 2000 album A Thousand Miles or More. They noted:

This song is an interesting dialogue between mother and daughter. On the occasion of her 18th birthday, this young woman decides she would like a fella. At this request, her mother does the “when I was your age” spiel in fear of her daughter losing her…purity. After much pleading and cheekiness, the daughter is granted her birthday wish, with reluctant parental consent. The echoes of the past here are surprisingly strong and familiar.
