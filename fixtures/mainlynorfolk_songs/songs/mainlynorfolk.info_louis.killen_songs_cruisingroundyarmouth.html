Cruising Round Yarmouth

[ Roud 2432 ; Ballad Index RcCRYar ; DT CRUISYAR , CRUISYA2 ; Mudcat 42245 ; trad.]

Harry Cox of Catfield, Norfolk, sang Cruising Round Yarmouth on 2 December 1953 to Alan Lomax. This recording was included on the album Songs of Seduction (The Folk Songs of Britain Vol. 2, Caedmon 1961; Topic 1968) but not on the extended Rounder CD reissue that was published in 2000. A longer recording with one and two half more verses, made by Peter Kennedy in the 1950s, was included in 1994 on the Saydisc anthology of traditional English sea songs and shanties from the last days of sail, Sea Songs and Shanties. The first album芒聙聶s notes commented:

There are, however, other songs of this type, including The Roving Kind, Maggie May (Roud 1757), The Cruise of the Calebar and the most obscene of all, The Good Ship Venus. None of these can compare in aptness of imagery, in simplicity and strength of language to this masterpiece from Harry Cox of Norfolk.

Ewan MacColl sang While Cruising Around Yarmouth in 1957 on his and A.L. Lloyd芒聙聶s Tradition Records album Blow Boys Blow. A.L. Lloyd noted:

This ballad, as rough as sailcloth and as sharp-flavoured as salt-horse, probably began its life among the “flying-fish sailors” who voyaged to the Orient in East Indiamen and Blackwall frigates. The men in the Western Ocean packets learned this song too, but they changed it into a shanty, well-known as Blow the Man Down. The present version, which pulls no punches, is from one of the best of English folk singers, Harry Cox of Catfield, Norfolk.

Sam Larner of Winterton, Norfolk, sang Cruising Round Yarmouth in 1958 to Ewan MacColl, Peggy Seeger and/or Charles Parker. This recording was included in 2014 as the title track on Larner芒聙聶s Musical Traditions anthology Cruising Round Yarmouth. Rod Stradling noted:

This song seems to be the almost exclusive property of Harry Cox, whose first line “While cruising round Yarmouth one day for a spree” has given the song its title. Roud has only 15 entries, all from England except for a couple of Doerflinger collections in the USA.

Louis Killen sang Cruising Round Yarmouth at a midnight folk concert recorded in London in May 1963 for the Decca album Hootenanny in London. He also recorded it in 1995 for his CD Sailors, Ships & Chanteys. He noted:

From the “low end” of Sam Larner芒聙聶s repertoire, which had many boisterous songs filled with humorous innuendo. In this one, the humour is increased by the use of shipboard terms to describe behaviour and anatomy, a practise used in many sailors songs.

Cyril Tawney recorded Cruising Round Yarmouth in 1989 for his maritime cassette of songs of seafarers and the fairer sex, Sailor芒聙聶s Delight. This track was also included in 2007 on his 2 CD anthology The Song Goes On. He noted:

A bit of bawdry from the singing of Harry Cox. Peter Bellamy, who featured the song in his set for many years, described it as an exercise in single entendre. It belongs to that genre of songs where precise vocabulary and references to some artisan practice are applied to sexual activity.
