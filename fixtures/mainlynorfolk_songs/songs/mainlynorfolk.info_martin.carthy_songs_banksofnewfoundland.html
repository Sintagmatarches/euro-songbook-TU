Ewan MacColl sang The Banks of Newfoundland in 1957 on his and A.L. Lloydâs album of sea songs, Blow Boys Blow. Lloyd noted:

In winter, the westward run from Liverpool to New York was a hard trip for packet ships, through heavy seas, contrary winds, sleet and snow. The large crews were kept busy reefing as the gales increased or piling on canvas whenever the wind abated. The mate drove the men, the skipper drove the mate, and the company drove the skipper. Willie Scott sang The Banks of Newfoundland in a recording made by Bill Leader in his studio in Elland, Yorkshire, on 13 February 1976. It was included in 1998 on the Topic anthology My Ship Shall Sail the Ocean (The Voice of the People Series Volume 2).

Chris Foster sang Banks of Newfoundland in 1977 on his Topic album Layers and in 2004 on his Tradition Bearers CD Jewels on which he noted:

The Banks of Newfoundland (collected from John Farr of Gwithian, Cornwall in 1926) is a great chorus song. It tells in vivid language of some of the hardships of crossing the Atlantic in the days of sail, when it was a much tougher and riskier business than it is today, notwithstanding international terrorism.

Tish Stubbs and Sam Richards sang The Banks of Newfoundland in 1977 on their Saydisc album Invitation to North America. They noted:

One of the most important sources of contact between Britain and North America in the 19th century revolved around merchant shipping. Packet ships raced across the western ocean and back in a mad dash to corner the markets. Fortunes were made on the backs of the sailors who manhandled these mostly unsavoury vessels through sleet, snow, and storm.

Our song describes one of the roughest parts of a much sailed sea route from Liverpool to New York. Incidentally, it wasnât only sailors on board. Dan Milner sang Banks of Newfoundland with Louis Killen joining in on chorus in 1989 on his Topic album Irish Ballads & Songs of the Sea.

Warp Four sang The Banks of Newfoundland in 1996 on their CD with Liam Clancy, One Hundred Years Ago.

Danny Spooner sang The Banks of Newfoundland on his 2002 CD Launch Out on the Deep. He noted:

The North Atlantic in winter can be a frightening bit of water. For the packet rat life was tough. The weather was often murderous with big seas, freezing winds and ice on the sails and in the rigging. Companies drove the skippers, skippers drove the mates and the mates drove the sailors. Peter Shepheard sang Banks of Newfoundland in 2005 on Shepheard, Spiers & Watsonâs Springthyme album They Smiled As We Cam In. He noted:

This is one of my favourite songs and I seem never to have tired of it since I first recorded it from St Andrews fisherman Tom Gordon in 1966. He learned it in turn from a man who had sailed on the whaler fleet out of Leith in the early 1900s. This is the only version I have come across that is modernised into the steam boat era—and incidentally dated in the text to 1906.

The Grand Banks of Newfoundland were famed for their productivity although the fishery has recently declined possibly caused by overfishing or by changes in water temperature brought about by global warming. The harsh winter weather made the task very hard and the men were only too happy to “bid farewell to the Virgin Rocks of Newfoundland” and bring the season to a close with a trip past Sandy Bay and on to New York.

False Lights sang The Banks of Newfoundland live at Folk East Festival, Suffolk, on 17 August 2014, which was released in the same year on their CD Live at Folk East. They also recorded it for their 2015 CD Salvor.

John Jones sang Banks of Newfoundland in 2015 on his Westpark album Never Stop Moving.

Siobhan Miller sang Banks of Newfoundland on her 2017 album Strata. It was voted as best traditional track at the BBC 2 Folk Awards 2018.
