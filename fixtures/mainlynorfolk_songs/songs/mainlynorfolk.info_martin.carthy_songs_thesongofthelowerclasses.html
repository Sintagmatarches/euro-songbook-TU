The Song of the Lower Classes / Song of the Other Ranks

[ Roud V39520 ; Mudcat 19019 ; words Ernest Jones]

Martin Carthy sang The Song of the Lower Classes a cappella with multi-tracked vocals on his 1982 album Out of the Cut. This track was re-released in 1993 on The Collection, and it is also on the minersâ benefit compilation album of 1993, Undefeated. Martin Carthy noted on the original album:

It was courtesy of Vic [Gammon] that I was able to hang around the library of the Sussex Archaeological Society in Lewes of which he is a member, and look through several of the hand-written hymnals of the 18th and 19th centuries. In one presented by an Uckfield gentleman and inscribed “G.J. Baker 1813” there was a three-part hymn, Otford, and later on it seemed appropriate to sing to it the words of the 19th-century Chartist Ernest Jones, The Song of the Lower Classes. If it seems extraordinary that words written 130 years ago should sound loud and clear today, then a small investigation of other writings of the period (1840s and 1850s) will show that it is by no means alone in that. Sometimes the comparisons are indeed arresting.

The writer, Ernest Jones, stood unsuccessfully as a Chartist MP in 1847, was arrested in 1848 and sentenced to two years of solitary confinement. From 1851 on, he started publishing a weekly magazine, Notes to the People, in which this song was published in March 1852.

Bob Davenport sang this song as Song of the Other Ranks, adding two verses of his own and having pinched the tune from Sergei Prokofiev, on his 2004 Topic CD The Common Stone.

Finest Kind sang Song of the Lower Classes on their 2010 album For Honour & for Gain. They noted:

Martin Carthy first drew Ian [Robb]âs attention to this poem, written by Englishman Ernest Jones in the early 1850s. Jones was a vocal member of the Chartist movement and an admirer of Marx and Engels, and in 1848 he was sentenced to two years, mostly spent in solitary confinement, for “seditious” speeches. While in jail, he was denied the use of pen and paper, and is reputed to have written some of his work in his own blood on the torn-out pages of a prayer book. The Chartist movement lasted only a few years, but is often credited with creating a new working class consciousness in England, which led ultimately to the labour movement and increased political clout for the unprivileged. Shelley [Posen] felt that in hindsight, Jonesâs rather dark and hopeless poem deserved a more uplifting conclusion, so Ian wrote one. While at it, he also came up with a new melody for Finest Kind to sink its teeth into.

Ewan McLennan sang The Song of the Lower Classes in 2014 on his Fellside album Stories Still Untold. He noted:

Ernest Charles Jones was one of the leading members, speakers and authors of the British Chartist movement during the mid-19th century. In 1848 he was sentenced to two years solitary confinement for making seditious public speeches, a time he purportedly used to write a great number of poems and polemics. On his release he began publishing a weekly newspaper—always with excerpts of songs, poetry or prose—and the first appearance of the text of this song was there in March 1852, published as poem.
