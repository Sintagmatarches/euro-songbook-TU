Child has only one version, from Anna Gordon, Mrs Brown of Falkland in 1783. Greig-Duncan has one fragment recited by Bell Robertson of New Pitsligo, Aberdeenshire in 1906, Simon芒聙聶s Lady, and quotes:

Though Danish and Swedish versions of this ballad are numerous, only one British source for it has hitherto been known—Mrs Brown of Falkland, from whose dictation about 1783 two copies extant in manuscript, and not greatly differing, are derived. These were published, with unauthorised changes, by Scott and Jamieson respectively, under the titles of Willie芒聙聶s Lady and Sweet Willy, and are given in their correct form by Child. Our version, unfortunately fragmentary, is parallel to Mrs Brown芒聙聶s, but independent. The first part of the ballad, here wanting, relates how Willie (in our version Simon) married a wife against the will of his mother, who was “a vile rank witch of the vilest kind”. When it comes to the birth of the daughter-in-law芒聙聶s child, the witch uses all her supernatural power to prevent it. Several gifts are offered to her successively that she may remove her spell, but her repeated answer is similar to what is here given once (1-3). But the Billy Blin芒聙聶 (a kindly household spirit appearing in other ballads) suggests a plan whose object is to betray the mother-in-law by leading her to believe that her enchantments have already been thwarted (4-7). Then in her wrath she unwittingly reveals the charms she had trusted to, and so enables Willie (Simon) to break them (11-14).

The interest of the present fragment is that we have here a second and independent version of a ballad hitherto known in our language only from Mrs Brown芒聙聶s dictation a hundred and thirty years ago. Miss Bell Robertson learnt the ballad from her mother, and never even heard it referred to by any other person; and she has good reason to believe that it came from her grandmother, whose songs were learnt in the eighteenth century or the very beginning of the nineteenth, before the publication by Scott or Jamieson could have any effect on such singers. The internal evidence, besides, indicates independence. The first half rather suggests a briefer form; and though the thought is the same, most of the lines are verbally different. The second half, however, is still more distinct, all the three charms and their undoing being quite different from those in the other version. It is an independent traditional version, though it is interesting to remember that Mrs Brown also learnt her ballads in Aberdeenshire.
(D, Last Leaves MS, 790.5/2/3, pp. 23.5)

Ray Fisher sang Willie芒聙聶s Lady as the title track of her 1982 Folk-Legacy album, Willie芒聙聶s Lady. She noted:

I have set this magnificent ballad to a tune of a Breton drinking song [Son Ar Chistr or The Song of Cider]. The text is based entirely on the contents in Francis James Child芒聙聶s massive collection, The English and Scottish Popular Ballads. I have omitted, added, and ‘telescoped’ some of the verses.

For immediate understanding, the plot is as follows: Willie marries a young and beautiful girl. His mother, a witch, disapproves of the girl and curses her. The girl will never produce a child; she and the child will die in childbirth. Offers of gifts to the mother to lift the curse prove fruitless. Willie seeks and gets help from the servant, the Billy Blind. Willie follows the Billy Blind芒聙聶s instructions and foils his mother芒聙聶s scheme and eventually fathers a son.

The Billy Blind: Some Scottish households retained a non-working servant who possesses some disability, e.g. deaf, dumb, hare-lipped or blind. The belief was held that they had second-sight, wisdom, or some supernatural power to compensate for their disability. They were feared by many, mainly due to ignorance. A blind man may well develop an extra keen hearing capacity and a refined sense of touch, so the belief was reasonably well-founded. Thus, as a means of protection or insurance against evil, a household would shelter such a person. In this ballad he was blind.

A brief clarification of the curses:
The knots in the girl芒聙聶s hair (note the magic number, nine; 3 × 3 = powerful) symbolise the constricting elements—holding back the free-flowing birth of the child. Even today, in some parts of Scotland, during childbirth a girl芒聙聶s garments are loose, unbuttoned, without pins or fastenings.
The combs (kaims o芒聙聶 care) of care were pressed through the long, golden hair, accompanied by a curse each time, and then left in the hair to hold in the curse. The hair is a powerful vehicle for curse-making.
The master kid (a young goat) was the link between the forces of evil and the witch—the catalyst or carrier. This invariably is an animal—the witch芒聙聶s cat being the most widely-known example.
The woodbine is a clinging, constricting plant that holds on and winds around other plants and branches—holding in again is symbolised here.
Lastly, the left-side shoe (leften shee) again has evil influence (i.e., Latin: sinister). This was tightly knotted to strengthen the curse.
Finally, the advice from the Billy Blind to make a wax baby and invite the mother to the christening is a master stroke indeed. This results in the eventual birth of a son.

The mother really laid it on pretty heavily with the curses—any one would have done the trick! She must either have doubted her own skills or have feared the power of the love bond between her son and the girl.
