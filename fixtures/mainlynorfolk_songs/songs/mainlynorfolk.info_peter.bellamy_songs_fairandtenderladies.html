Fair and Tender Ladies / Little Sparrow

[ Roud 451 ; Ballad Index R073 ; VWML CJS2/10/3425 ; Folkinfo 121 ; DT FAIR&TEN ; Mudcat 19342 ; trad.]

Note: The Traditional Ballad Index lists G/D 6:1156 with this song while Steve Roud gave it its own number Roud 6820 .

Alasdair Clayre: 100 Folk Songs and New Songs Jean Ritchie: Folk Songs of the Southern Appalachians Cecil Sharp, Maud Karpeles: English Folk Songs From the Southern Appalachians

Jean Ritchie sang The Little Sparrow in 1954 on her Elektra album Kentucky Mountain Songs and, as Come All You Fair and Tender Ladies, in 1965 on her Warner album A Time for Singing. She noted on the first album:

The Little Sparrow or, Fair and Tender Ladies, as it is sometimes called, is to me one of the incomparable love songs of all time. It is widely known throughout our part of the Appalachians, and even within our own family is sung in two or three different tunes and which varying sets of words. This particular way of singing it, which I prefer, comes from Uncle Jason.

Jean Ritchie, Oscar Brand and David Sear also sang a medley of Fair and Tender Ladies on their 1959 Folkways live album A Folk Concert in Town Hall, New York. Brandâs introductory comment was:

In preparing a program of this sort, one of the major problems is selecting which version we should sing together. This often occurs when we record our albums. Instead of solving this problem, each one of us will sing his own version!

And Jean Ritchie sang Come All You Fair and Tender Ladies in 1965 on her Warner album A Time for Singing on which she noted:

“The sad ones are the lovely-est”, old people round home have often said to me. Our old love lament is one of the best examples of this statement that I know. When I learnt it from Uncle Jason, he was getting on in years and he wasnât too sure of the tune, singing it a bit differently on each verse, and forgetting a line of words here and there. Over the years I have adapted it to my own way of singing, so that this present tune is almost completely my own, although it is reminiscent of his in feeling. That will never change.

Peggy Seeger sang the Appalachian love song Come All Ye Fair and Tender Maidens in 1957 on her 10" Topic LP Eleven American Ballads and Songs, reissued in 1996 on the Fellside CD Classic Peggy Seeger. Alan Lomax noted:

This classic Appalachian love song takes the view, which is unusual in American love songs, that love is both sorrowful and dangerous.

A year later, Pete Seeger sang this song with the more usual title Come All Ye Fair and Tender Ladies on his Topic EP Pete and Five Strings. Karl Dallas commented in the sleeve notes:

Sharp printed 18 versions of this beautiful song [in English Folk Songs From the Southern Appalachians], including a version from one of his best sources, Mrs. Jane Gentry of Hot Springs, North Carolina. Compare Peteâs very free interpretation with sister Peggyâs more rhythmic performance.

Hedy West sang Fair and Tender Ladies in 1964 on her Vanguard album Hedy West Volume 2. She noted:

Fair and Tender Ladies is one of twelve songs I recorded, in the summer of 1960 from the late Lula Prather Denson of Pickens County, Georgia. (Another one is Sweet Jane on [Hedy West].) In addition to having a fine voice, Mrs. Denson had selected good variants to commit to memory. I regret that more of her songs were not recorded before she died in 1961.

Sara Cleveland of Brant Lake, New York, sang Come All You Maidens to Sandy Paton in 1965. This recording was included in 1968 on her Folk-Legacy album Ballads & Songs of the Upper Hudson Valley. Kenneth S. Goldstein noted:

Lyric folksongs (those expressing moods and feelings rather than telling stories) are frequently difficult to identify as being one or another specific song as they consist of a number of images or symbols sometimes appearing in one combination and at other times in other combinations. Usually composed of a string of folk commonplaces, they borrow from a stock of floating motifs. Sometimes, however, we find a rather stable combination of three or four of these making up a recognisable unitary song. Such is the case with this song, more commonly known as Little Sparrow or Fair and Tender Ladies. Beginning with the “Come all ye…” opening, it proceeds to compare inconstant suitors with stars on a summerâs morning, tells the maidenâs sad story of her mistreatment by a false lover, and then closes with her wishing she were a swallow (or sparrow) so she could fly to him and seek out the reasons for his deluding ways.

The song has been collected widely throughout the Southern Mountains and other parts of the South from Florida to Oklahoma; this, to my knowledge, is the first time it has been reported from a northern traditional singer. Sara learned it from her mother, and comments: “I donât think my mother had a particularly happy life, and she liked to sing sad songs like this.”

Originally an English street song, taken to Ireland and from there to the Appalachian Mountains of America. The version which has come back to us from America forms the basis of this modern day presentation of the song.
