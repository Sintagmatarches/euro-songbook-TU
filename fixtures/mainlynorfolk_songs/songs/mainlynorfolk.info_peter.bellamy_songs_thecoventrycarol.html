Iontach sang the Coventry Carol on their 2008 Christmas album Flight of the Wren. They noted:

The Coventry Carol dates from the 16th century. It was performed as part of the Coventry Mystery Plays, a cycle of medieval mystery plays from Coventry, West Midlands, England. The play, called The Pageant of the Shearmen and Tailors, depicts the Christmas story as told in the gospel of Matthew whilst the carol presents the Massacre of the Innocents in which Herod orders the killing of all male infants in Bethlehem. The lyrics of this haunting carol tell of a motherâs lament for her doomed child. It is the only carol that has survived from this play and is a noted example of a Picardy third or Tierce de Picardie. The author is unknown but the oldest known text was written by Robert Croo in 1534, and the oldest known printing of the melody dates back to 1591. Lyrics

The Young Tradition sing The Coventry Carol

Lully, lullay, thou little tiny child
By, by, lully, lullay
Lully, lullay, thou little tiny child
By, by, lully, lullay

Oh sisters two, how may we do
For to preserve this day?
This poor youngling ,for whom we do sing,
“By, by, lully, lullay.”

Herod the king in his raging
Charged he hath this day
His men of might, in his own sight
All young children to slay.

That woe is me, poor child, for thee,
And ever morn and day1,
For thy parting nor say nor sing,
“By, by, lully, lullay.”

1 Tim McElwaine told me that “morn and day” is from The Oxford Book of Carols. The New Oxford Book of Carols has “And ever morne and say”, noting this means ‘grieve and sigh’.
