Harry Cox of Catsfield, Norfolk, recorded The Pretty Ploughboy at the Decca Studios, London, for the EFDSS in 1934. It was released on a 78rpm disc then, and was included in 1998 both on the EFDSS anthology A Century of Song and on the Topic anthology My Ship Shall Sail the Ocean (The Voice of the People Volume 2). Another recording, made by Peter Kennedy in London in December 1953, was published in 2000 on Cox芒聙聶s Rounder CD What Will Become of England?. A third version, recorded by Charles Parker and Ewan MacColl in the mid-1960s, was included in 2000 too on Cox芒聙聶s Topic anthology The Bonny Labouring Boy. Steve Roud noted on the latter album:

A somewhat conventional story-line of faithful and persistent love has not prevented this being an extremely popular song, with versions collected all over England, Scotland, and Ireland, and others in Canada and the USA. The Edwardian collectors, such as Sharp, Merrick, Greig, and Vaughan Williams noted it often, and more recent recordings include those from Walter Pardon and Fred Jordan. It was equally popular on broadsides, with examples from nearly all the well-known 19th century printers, including Catnach and Pitts, which prove its existence in the 1820s, but there is no evidence of it being any older.

Bill Whaley and Dave Fletcher sang The Pretty Ploughboy, and Bruce Molsky sang The Simple Ploughboy, in 2005 on the Fellside anthology of English traditional songs and their American variants, Song Links 2. Paul Adams noted on the first song:

The Pretty Ploughboy was a popular broadside ballad in the 1800s, and was still widely sung a hundred years later, several versions being collected all across the country, and all with very fine tunes. The press-gangs were hated throughout the countryside in the 18th and early 19th centuries for taking away young men to serve in the army or the navy, and the popularity of this song must surely have been because love triumphs, the ploughboy saved by the endeavours of his sweetheart. Dave and Bill芒聙聶s version is from Harry Cox of Norfolk. Other press-gang songs, with a less optimistic ending are All Things Are Quite Silent, collected by Vaughan Williams in 1904 from Ted Baines of Lower Beeding, Sussex, and the High Germanie family. By 1815 the press-gangs were being disbanded, and by 1835, this cruel and brutal method of recruiting had ended.
