Down by the Seaside

[ Roud 1712 / Song Subject MAS285 ; Ballad Index RcDBTSS ; trad.]

George ‘Pop’ Maynard sang Down by the Seaside at home in Copthorne, Sussex, on 3 December 1955. This recording made by Peter Kennedy was released in 1976 on Maynardâs Topic album traditional songs from Sussex, Ye Subjects of England and was included in 2012 on the Topic anthology of songs by Southern English traditional singers, You Never Heard So Sweet (The Voice of the People Volume 21). Another version recorded by Brian Matthews at The Cherry Tree, Copthorne, on 16 December 1959 was included in 2000 on Popâs Musical Traditions anthology Down the Cherry Tree. A third version, recorded by Brian Matthews at The Cherry Tree too, but on 18 May 1960, was included in 2001 on the Musical Traditions anthology of 1960 songs from Sussex country pubs, Just Another Saturday Night. Rod Stradling commented in the enclosed booklet:

It would appear that this lovely song has appeared in not a single broadside or book in the past. The only Roud entries for this song are the two 1955 and â56 Peter Kennedy recordings of Pop Maynard singing it, the latter of which appeared on the Topic LP Ye Subjects of England. This is surprising in the light of the notes to that album, where Mike Yates puts it in a list of several of Popâs songs described as being “stock Victorian broadside ballads, albeit ones with ancient histories even then”. Mike now tells me that Down by the Seaside is included in a chapbook printed c.1820 by J. Fraser of Stirling as The Sailorâs Loss, and says that the internal repeat suggests a late 18th century Stage or Pleasure Garden origin.

Shirley Collins sang Down by the Seaside on her 1974 Topic album, Adieu to Old England. It was also included on her anthologies Fountain of Snow, Within Sound and The Classic Collection, and on the Topic double CD of 1999, The Folk Collection. A.L. Lloyd noted on the original album:

This gentle and unashamedly romantic broadside relic is from George Maynard of Copthorne, Sussex.

Andy Turner sang Down by the Seaside in 1990 on his cassette Love, Death and the Cossack, and as the 5 January 2014 entry of his project A Folk Song a Week. He noted on his cassette:

Marbles champion George Maynard lived at Copthorne on the Sussex/Surrey border. Down by the Seaside was one of many fine songs in his repertoire.

Matt Quinn learned Down by the Seaside from the singing of Pop Maynard and recorded it for his 2017 CD The Brighton Line. He noted:

This one has everything—romance, sailing ships, death and even a unique (happy to be proved wrong) mention of an opera-glass!. This song appears to be collected only from Pop Maynard.

Nick Dow sang Down by the Seaside on his 2018 album of unaccompanied traditional folk songs, Far and Wide. He noted:

From the singing of Pop Maynard. I learned it from those treasured recordings along with everybody else who sings it. Very little is known about the song except that Down by the Seaside is included in a chapbook printed c.1820 by J. Fraser of Stirling as The Sailorâs Loss, and shouts an 18th century origin. Note that there is another song Down by the Seaside (Roud 2733), which is of the returned-token variety, and of course the narrator in the end turns out to be the maidâs lost lover. Frank Harte of Dublin sang this song as The Love Token on his album My Name Is Napoleon Bonaparte; Dervish learned it from him and sang it as The Loverâs Token in 2013 on their album The Thrush in the Storm.

Culverake sang Down by the Seaside on their 2025 album Unto the Sky. They noted:

There are just 24 entries for this song in the Roud Index, and they are all credited to international marbles champion (1948) George ‘Pop’ Maynard, from Copthorne, Sussex. Possibly descended from a song called The Sailorâs Loss, printed in chapbook c.1820 by J Fraser of Stirling. The only traditional song we know of that mentions opera glasses.
