Rigs of the Time

[ Roud 876 / Song Subject MAS852 ; Ballad Index K237 ; Wiltshire 26 ; DT RIGSTIME ; Mudcat 93871 ; trad.]

‘Rig’ means, according to the OED, ‘a trick or way of swindling someone’. There are a lot of versions of this song and there always seems to be a cause for new verses.

A traditional version of the well known song Rigs of the Time (see Kennedy, Folksongs of Britain and Ireland) was sung by John ‘Charger’ Salmons and friends in an October 1947 recording by E.J. Moeran for the BBC at the Sutton Windmill pub, near Stalham, Norfolk. This was broadcast on the BBC Third Programme in late 1947 and published in 1955 on the Alan Lomax collection album World Library of Folk and Primitive Music: England. and in 2000 on the Snatchâd From Oblivion CD East Anglia Sings. Shirley Collins sang The Rigs of the Time with three more verses and with a chorus of the Young Tradition on her 1967 album The Sweet Primeroses. She cites Charger Salmonsâ recording as her source. This track was also included in 2009 on Topicâs 70th anniversary anthology Three Score and Ten, and in 2014 on the GFTU/Topic anthology of songs of resistance, democracy and peace, Voice and Vision.

Harry Cox sang Rigs of the Times in a recording made in Catfield, Norfolk in 1967 on his 2000 Topic anthology The Bonny Labouring Boy.

Sandy Denny sang an unaccompanied version of Rigs of the Time as an encore of her Eltham Well Hall Open Theatre concert on 6 August 1972 with her then band of Richard Thompson, Pat Donaldson and Timi Donald. An audience sound recording was available as a bootleg album, but no official recording is known.

Mike Herring sang Rigs of the Times in 1972 on the Topic/Impact album of songs and ballads of the agricultural labourer, The Painful Plough, that was released to accompany Roy Palmerâs book of the same name, The Painful Plough. Roy Palmer noted:

Now a rare song, this was once common on broadsides in the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, often ending with a jibe at balloonists, which perhaps sheds light on the origin of our last verse. E.J. Moeran collected this version in Norfolk in 1947.

Vic Harrup sang The Rigs of the Time in a “sing-song” at The Ship Inn, Blaxhall, Suffolk on 16 November 1973. The recording of this event was released in 1974 on the Transatlantic album The Larks They Sang Melodious.

Peter Bellamy sang Rigs of the Time in 1975 on his eponymous LP Peter Bellamy, now available as part of the Fellside 2 CD compilation Fair Annie. He noted on his original album:

Martin Carthy refreshed Rigs of the Time with his own verses on his 1982 album Out of the Cut; this was re-released in 1993 on Rigs of the Time: The Best of Martin Carthy. Martin Carthy noted on the original album:

I had been singing Rigs of the Time for ten years or so before thinking of verses that did something other than update price-increases. For the benefit of people not acquainted with British current affairs, ‘Peach’ is Blair Peach, who died of a fractured skull at an anti-fascist rally which was policed by the S.P.G.
