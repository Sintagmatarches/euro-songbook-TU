Alison Gross / Alison Cross

[ Roud 3212 / Song Subject MAS191 ; Child 35 ; Ballad Index C035 ; DT ALIGROSS ; Mudcat 25270 , 153463 ; trad.]

John Laurie recited the Child ballad Alison Gross in 1959 on the anthology The Jupiter Book of Ballads.

Ewan MacColl sang Allison Gross in 1967 on the Argo album Poetry and Song Vol. 5.

Dave and Toni Arthur sang Alison Gross in 1970 on their Trailer album Hearken to the Witches Rune, set to a melody that seems to be a modified (modal) form of the tune Cecil Sharp found in Somerset with The Knight and the Shepherd芒聙聶s Daughter, including a form of the refrain: “Line, twine, the willow dee” [Mudcat Caf脙漏 note from Malcolm Taylor].

Steeleye Span recorded Alison Gross, translated from the original Scots, shortened, arranged and set to the tune Down in Yon Forest by Bob Johnson, for their 1973 album Parcel of Rogues. A live recording from the Royal Opera Theatre in Adelaide, Australia in 1982 was released on their rare Australia-only LP On Tour and in 2001 on their CD Gone to Australia.

Folque did a wonderful Norwegian version of Alison Gross in 1974 on their eponymous debut album, Folque.

Jon Rennard sang Alison Gross in 1975 on the Traditional Sound cassette Wayside Tradition. This track was included in 2015 as a bonus track of the digital release of his 1971 Traditional Sound album The Parting Glass.

Lizzie Higgins sang Alison Cross in 1985 on her Lismor album What a Voice. The liner notes commented:

A haunting, fanciful ballad of magic and transformation. Alison Cross came to Lizzie through her father, who learned it from his mother. The ballad has many Scandinavian analogues.

An earlier recording of Lizzie Higgins singing Alison Gross (note spelling!), made by Peter Hall at the Jeannie Robertson Memorial Concert in 1977, was released in 2006 on her Musical Traditions anthology In Memory of Lizzie Higgins. Rod Stradling noted in the accompanying booklet:

The text originally appeared in Jamieson, Popular Ballads and Songs 2 [1806], pp.187-190, said to have been collected from Mrs Anna Brown, of Falkland, Aberdeen, in 1792-1794; the only collection from the oral tradition.

Lizzie was deeply interested in the interfaces between the worlds of ordinary folk, of those who practised the Black Arts in league with the Devil, and—as in this ballad—of those of the Scottish fairies, an amoral, unpredictable tribe of whom Stanley Robertson says, “We call them the Guid Folk—because they can dee you an awful lot of damage” (Barrie芒聙聶s dangerously vicious Tinker Bell being a prime depiction). The hero of the song can count himself lucky that the Queen of the Fairies took pity on him, even as a much-desired witch芒聙聶s pet (although, as Child observed, the Scottish Queens are reputed to be more favourably inclined to help humans enchanted by their subjects). Stanley Robertson comments that Lizzie started singing this late in life and probably derived it from print.

Elspeth Cowie learned Alison Cross from Lizzie Higgins when she lived in Aberdeen. She sang it as member of the band Seannachie on the 1994 Greentrax anthology of songs from the Tron Tavern, Edinburgh, Ceilidh House Sessions, and on her 2000 CD Naked Voice. She also sang it at Celtic Connections at the Glasgow Royal Concert Hall in January 2001. This concert was released a year later on the Greentrax CD Scots Women.

Judy Cook sang Alison Gross on her 1998 album If You Sing Songs…. She noted:

Learned from Steeleye Span. I leave out the heavy rock beat and electric guitars, and add back in a few verses from Child where it is ballad #35. I find the tune, when smoothed out, is remarkably similar to the traditional carol Down in Yon Forest from The Oxford Book of Carols.

Malinky sang Alison Cross in 2000 on their Greentrax album Last Leaves. This track was also included in 2007 on the WildGoose anthology Songs of Witchcraft and Magic. A live recording from Glasgow Royal Concert Hall at Celtic Connections 2000 was included in 2019 on the bonus CD of their 20th anniversary album Handsel. They noted:

It芒聙聶s generally a mistake to say no to witches, especially if they try to buy you off as their lover (leman). This guy was really lucky the good fairie queen helped him out. [Alexander Keith芒聙聶s] Last Leaves refers to a version from [about] 1783 by a Mrs Brown and ours is a shortened version from the singing of the great Lizzie Higgins, which leaves you to wonder exactly what a thing-that-toddles-around-the-tree might be. Perish the thought.

Wendy Weatherby sang Alison Gross in 2010 on her Fellside CD A Shirt of Silk or Snow. She noted:

The ‘ugliest witch in the North Country’ wreaks her revenge on an unpersuadable and unwilling prospective lover by turning him into an ‘ugly worm’ (dragon). He is later rescued by the Queen of the Fairies. My sympathies lie with Alison, though!

Rapunzel and Sedayne sang Alison Cross in 2011 on the Woodbine & Ivy Band芒聙聶s CD The Woodbine & Ivy Band.

Judy Baker and Annie Grace sang and played Alison Gross in 2016 on the Greentrax anthology of pipe music and song from the Scottish Borders, Reclaimed.

Hannah Rarity sang Alison Cross, with lyrics very similar to Malinky芒聙聶s, on her 2018 CD Neath the Gloaming Star.
