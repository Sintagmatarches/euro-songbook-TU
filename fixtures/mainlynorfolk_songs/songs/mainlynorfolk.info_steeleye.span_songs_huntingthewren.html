John Kirkpatrick et al sang Hunting the Wren on the Folkworks project and subsequent 1998 Fellside CD Wassail!. He noted:

A song with echoes of some sort of sacrificial offering. On the day after Christmas it was the practice to parade around with a recently killed wren ceremonially displayed, with this as the accompanying song. In return for a contribution you were given a feather from the wren for good luck. When all the feathers were gone, you would have a feast, and then bury its bones, gaining strength from the King of the Birds.

Versions of considerable variation have been found all over the British Isles, and this one was recorded from Joe & Winifred Woods, of Douglas, Isle of Man, by Leslie Daiken in the 1940s.

Iontach sang the Wren Song Doubled on their 2008 Christmas album Flight of the Wren. They noted:

The hunting of the wren is celebrated on 26 December, Saint Stephens芒聙聶s Day, also known in Irish as L脙隆 an Dreoil脙颅n (the day of the wren). Originally the tradition involved a band of young boys who hunted down a wren, following it through ditches and hedges, over hill and dale until either the poor wren died from exhaustion or they managed to catch and kill it. Once the bird was dead, it was attached to a pole and the group would parade around town with it. It was during this procession that they would sing the wren song, of which there are many versions, asking for donations from the local townspeople. This could be in the form of money, food or drink. The patrons often received a feather from the bird for luck in return for their generosity. Monies raised were used to host a dance, known as a wren dance, at which traditional Irish music, song and dance would be the order of the day. Over the years, the killing of a wren has thankfully been abandoned and an effigy is used in its place. Wren boys have been joined by girls and adults and. depending on which region of Ireland you are in, are referred to as wrenboys, mummers or strawboys. Music still plays a major role in the celebration, and the wren song, or the wran song as it is mostly called, still lives on. This version came from Siobh脙隆n [Kennedy] who learned it from her late father, Rory Kennedy. At the beginning of the song you will hear a wren in action—hence the title of the track!

It is an Irish folk song sung traditionally by the Wren Boys with variations of the same tradition in France, England and the Isle of Man. The Wren Boys were a group of young lads dressed in masks and straw hats who would gather, hunt and kill a wren with a stick. They would hang the wren on the holly bush branch and march around town to beg a penny ‘to bury the wren’. Or if they didn芒聙聶t catch a real bird there would make a little effigy. At the end of the day, they would bury the birds with a penny and this was thought to end any bad fortune for the family and bring good luck for the new year. A brutal tradition that is thankfully now only remembered in song and dance.

The wren is one of the smallest bird and has been thought to be one of the wisest birds in Ireland. But why did the the poor wren get killed? Well, there芒聙聶s plenty of folklore: One story says there was a competition betwixt the birds of Ireland to see who could fly the farthest and the winner would become the king of the birds. Just before they set off, the tiny wren hid inside the eagle芒聙聶s feathers and hitched a ride. When the eagle had outflown the other birds and flown as high as it could, the wren popped out and flew even higher to declare I am ‘R脙颅 na n脙聣an’, the king of the birds. By outsmarting the eagle the wren was branded a trickster and become synonymous with bad luck. Another story refers to Saint Stephen, who was hiding from his enemies in a bush until a wren flew over and started chattering, giving away his hiding place and so St Stephen was stoned to death. Another story involves Cl脙颅odhna, the queen of of the S脙颅dhe of south Munster and goddess of the Otherworld. It was said she seduced young men to follow her into the sea where they then drowned. A protective charm could be used to destroy her, and so her only way to escape was to transform into a wren. So legend goes, as punishment for her misdeeds, she was forced to become a wren every Christmas and die by human hand on L脙隆 an Dreoil脙颅n. You can see why the poor wren became known for bad luck and betrayal.

Some folklorists suggest that L脙隆 an Dreoil脙颅n descended from the Winter Solstice celebrations, Grianstad an Gheimhridh, and if a druid found a wren at this time, they would be blessed with wisdom for the coming year.
