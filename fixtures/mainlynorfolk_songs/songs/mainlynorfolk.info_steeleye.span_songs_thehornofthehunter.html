Dave Winter (about 30), farm manager of Kay Green Farm, Egton Bridge, Yorkshire, sang The Horn of the Hunter in 1962 to Colin S. Wharton who collected songs in the North Riding of Yorkshire for his Leeds University Thesis ‘Folk Songs from the North Riding’. This recording was included in 2019 on the Musical Traditions anthology of songs from the Colin Wharton Collection, Songs of the North Riding.

Joe Thompson sang Horn of the Hunter in 1953 at either the Crown and Thistle, Rockcliffe, or the Plough Inn, Wreay. This recording was included in 1982 on the Reynard Records album Pass the Jug Round, reissued in 2001 on a Veteran CD. Sue Allan noted:

The Taverners Folk Group sang The Horn of the Hunter in 1974 on their Folk Heritage album Times of Old England. They noted:

John Peel is probably the most celebrated hunting man in history, thanks to John Woodcock Graves and his writing of the song, John Peel. However, when Peel died, an old friend wrote an ode to the death of Peel, and called it The Horn of the Hunter Is Silent. Much later still, the ode became a song to a man who was an even greater huntsman than Peel, his name was Joe Bowman. Joe lived in Ullswater and was the huntsman for that famous pack for many years. Joe killed more foxes than ever Peel did, and one of Joeâs most favourite hunting valleys was Mardale. In the year of 1919 Lord Lonsdale sold the valley to Manchester Corporation who received permission to flood it, and so the great reservoir called Haweswater came into being. The Dunn Bull Hotel, the church, and several farms disappeared under the waters. The ghost of old Joe and the lost valley of Mardale ar all here in this song.

Brian Dewhurst with Tom Tiddlerâs Ground sang The Horn of the Hunter in 1975 on their Folk Heritage album The Hunter and the Hunted. He noted:

I got the words and tune from John Eatock of Leigh in May 1970. This is my favourite song of John Peel. Over six feet tall, Peel kept his own pack of hounds so that he could go hunting when he wanted. He lived at Caldbeck, eight miles north-east of Bassenthwaite. He died in 1854 at the age of 78 and is buried in Caldbeck church yard.

Paul and Linda Adams sang The Horn of the Hunter in 1978 on their Fellside album Among the Old Familiar Mountains. They noted:

Next to John Peel this is the most famous of Cumbrian Hunting Songs. It was written by Jackson Gillbanks, a contemporary of John Peel, and has given rise to many other songs (we recorded one, John Peelâs Lament, on our previous album Country Hirings). We have added a couple of verses from other versions to give an all round picture of the characters. John Woodcock Graves, mentioned in the song, was the man who wrote Dâ Ye Ken John Peel and who emigrated to Tasmania where he swapped foxhunting for kangaroo hunting. In Cumbria youâll find none of the pink coated hunting gentry—it is a common folks activity, following the hounds on foot over the wild fell country and giving rise to common folk songs.

Dave Tomlinson and Richard Bell noted (in emails to me):

The song seems to be set around Keswick and Bassenthwaite lake because of the places mentioned: Skiddaw Fell, Keswick, Bewaldeth and the river Caldew can all be seen on my road atlas. But the old county of Cumberland—now named Cumbria—has many place names that do not appear on maps because of the wild and mountainous landscape. John Peel and his hunt travelled all over Cumberland and these place names are well known to the people of the area. John Peel was the greatest huntsman ever in England, he was Master of Foxhounds for over 40 years and was well loved by the people. There are more songs about him, mainly Do Ye Ken John Peel.

Some locations are not so obvious:

  1. Brightenflat, locally known as just Brighten, is an area of wild moorland with no population.
  2. Denton Fell, locally know as just Denton, is another area of wild and hilly country.

Note: The tune of this song is quite similar to the tune of Bellman. Bellman was one of John Peelâs famous hunting hounds.
