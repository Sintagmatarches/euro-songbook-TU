Here We Come A-Wassailing

[ Roud 209 ; Master title: The Yorkshire Wassail Song ; TYG 22 ; Ballad Index JRDF166 ; GlosTrad Roud 209 ; Folkinfo 228 , 393 ; DT WASSBUD , WASSCOME ; Mudcat 54659 ; trad.]

A group of singers in Birmingham sang Here We Come A-Wassailing in 1957 in the live Christmas Day broadcast on BBC Radio, Sing Christmas and the Turn of the Year.

Robert Abramson on harpsichord and and LaNoue Davenport on recorder played the tune of The Carnal and the Crane in 1959 on Jean Ritchieâs Tradition album Carols of All Seasons. She noted:

The Wassail Song has been sung by the Waits in England since the 17th century and most probably earlier; it is perhaps the most popular Christmas and New Years carol of all. One can imagine the Waits, or carollers, walking briskly about the village on a cold Christmas Eve. What better way to keep warm and jolly than to keep singing the rollicking Wassail Song, it makes one dance just to hear it! The words, not sung here but undoubtedly familiar to everybody, leave nothing to be desired. The various verses include blessings on the house and all therein, an identification of the singers as neighbors and friends (not daily beggars!), a request for refreshments (barley beer, cheese, Christmas loaf), a good-bye and the best wishes of the season, which, by the way, is our wish for yon:

Love and joy come to you,
And to you your wassail too,
And God bless you and send you
A happy New Year.

A live version from a Christmas radio programme recorded in December 1980 at Crathorne Hall, Crathorne, North Yorkshire, was published in 2005 on the CD A Yorkshire Christmas. A.L. Lloyd noted on the Watersonsâ album:

At midwinter, ploughing time is near. It was the time when reassuring heroes went dancing and singing through the village with wishes for good luck and increase. If the wishes were to take effect, it was essential that the villagers make some kind of sacrifice. So the perambulating spell-worker would be given food or, later. money. Latter-day luck-visitors, singing their carols for bud and blossom and “cider running out of every gutter-hole”, expected nothing more than a few coppers for a Christmas booze-up. The melody of this widespread wassail song is a member of a vast and ancient tune-family, scattered across Europe as far as the Balkans, usually associated with the rites of midwinter.

Coope Boyes & Simpson sang Here We Come A-Wassailing in 1998 on their No Masters CD A Garland of Carols, and Coope Boyes & Simpson, Fi Fraser, Jo Freya and Georgina Boyes sang George Dunnâs Wassail in 2005 on their No Masters CD Voices at the Door. They noted:

Wassailing—house-to-house visits with a box containing a doll or simply to bring good wishes—was a common and widespread custom in the northern part of England from at least the eighteenth to the early twentieth centuries. This version of one of the songs which accompanied the tradition is from the singing of George Dunn (1887-1975).

Mr Dunn was born in Quarry Bank in Staffordshire and lived there for most of his life. One of seven children, he started in full time work for a chainmaker at the age of 13—he was paid 3/6d (171/2p) for a 59 hour week. He retired—as superintendent of the proof house where the chains were tested—in 1959 at the age of 72.

His recollections of life in nineteenth and early twentieth century Staffordshire provide a detailed, warm but totally unsentimental picture of the times and his repertoire of songs and singing was exceptional. “The sheer quality of his singing is astonishing, particularly for an octogenarian,” wrote the folklorist and historian Roy Palmer, who recorded Mr Dunn in the 1970s. “The strength of his songs and the skill of his singing command immediate respect and humility in the listener. They are a living manifestation of the high art and long tradition of English folk music.”

Mr Dunnâs carol was, [Palmer] said, incomplete, so weâve added [two more verses] widely found in traditional versions of the Wassail song to the two that he remembered.
