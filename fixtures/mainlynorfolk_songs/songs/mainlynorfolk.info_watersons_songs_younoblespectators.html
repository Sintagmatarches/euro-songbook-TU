You Noble Spectator / Ye Noble Spectators

[ Roud 610 ; Master title: You Noble Spectator ; TYG 83 ; Ballad Index BrMa016 ; Folkinfo 249 ; trad.]

Lucy E. Broadwood, J.A. Fuller Maitland: English County Songs Cecil J. Sharp: The Sword Dances of Northern England

The Watersons sang Ye Noble Spectators in 1966 on their album A Yorkshire Garland. Like most of the tracks from this LP, it was re-released in 1994 on their CD Early Days. A.L. Lloyd noted:

[…] For some reason (some say, on account of the Danish settlements, others say itâs to do with the calling of iron and coal miners) parts of the Northeast, particularly North Yorkshire and Durham have preserved well the old midwinter ceremonial dances once attached to a primitive folk play designed to revive the life-giving sun just when it seemed to be on the verge of dying. Theyâre called sword dances but in fact itâs probable that the strips of wood or metal the dancers carry represent rather working tools than weapons of destruction. Traditionally the dancers wore disguise, represented folk heroes, and an essential part of the dance drama was the mimed killing and resurrection of a champion. The “captain” of the team had the responsibility of introducing the heroes with a “calling-on” song. Ye Noble Spectators was the calling-on song for the dancers of Kirby Malzeard, near Ripon. where Cecil Sharp stumbled on the “sword dance” in 1906. The champion who was “put to death” and raised again in Kirby took on the name and attributes of the Biblical Samson. The song was taken down nearly eighty years ago from an old labourer, Thomas Wood, by John Fawcett, a farm foreman of Breconboro near Thirsk, and printed by Lucy Broadwood in her English County Songs.

Bill Price sang the Kirkby Malzeard Calling On Song in 1972 on his Folk Heritage album The Fine Old Yorkshire Gentleman. He noted:

Recorded by Cecil J. Sharp in Sword Dances of Northern England (1906) and by tradition sung by the Captain of the Kirkby Malzeard Longsword Dancers. The six dancers in the team line up at right angles to the audience and the Captain, sword in hand, turns to the audience and introduces the dancers with this song, finishing with [see below].

John Kirkpatrick et al. performed the Sword Dance Song & Tune in 1998 on their Fellside CD celebrating an English Midwinter, Wassail!.

Jess and Richard Arrowsmith sang their Kirkby Malzeard Raspberry Song in 2010 on their Hallamshire Traditions album of nursery songs, rhymes and lullabies, Off We Go!. They noted:

Traditional longsword calling on song, without the raspberries… this one bears a little explanation—whilst driving home from sword dance practice, Carmel and Jess came up with this variation on the calling on song as a way of keeping Lucy entertained. Lucy became a fan of blowing raspberries from about 6 weeks old.
